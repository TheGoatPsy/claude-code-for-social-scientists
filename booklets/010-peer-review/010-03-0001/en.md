# AI in Peer Review, Confidentiality, Evaluation, and Boundaries

When a peer review invitation arrives, a trust contract takes shape. A journal places an unpublished manuscript in the care of a researcher's judgment. That manuscript belongs to no one yet in any public sense: it has not been defended, circulated, or submitted to the scrutiny of a field. It is the author's unresolved thinking. The access the reviewer receives is temporary, purpose-bound, and subject to a confidentiality obligation.

AI tools have introduced a new variable into this picture. A researcher can now consult a cloud model, pose methodological questions, generate a draft report structure, or have the tone of a paragraph refined. Some of these possibilities are legitimate. But where does the line fall? Which uses honour the peer review contract, and which undermine it? This booklet addresses that question directly. Ten sections examine the confidentiality boundary in AI-assisted review, journal policies, legitimate and illegitimate uses, the responsibility of evaluation, report tone, communication with the editor, disclosure discipline, training for early-career researchers, and the outputs of the supporting skill. No section imposes a blanket ban on AI use. Every section asks: how far?

## 1. Confidentiality in Peer Review

A submitted manuscript is unpublished scientific labour. A reviewer receives it for evaluation; they may not use it for another purpose, incorporate it into their own research, or share it with others. This principle is not new. It forms the foundational layer of international academic publishing norms, and many journals articulate it explicitly in their reviewer guidelines.

Uploading manuscript text to a third-party AI tool raises a serious problem within this framework. Text sent to a cloud model may reach the model's training infrastructure, the service provider's servers, or potentially unknown third parties. Where the text is stored, how long it is retained, and who can access it cannot in most cases be verified by the reviewer. That uncertainty can make it practically impossible to honour the confidentiality obligation.

Resnik and Elmore (2016) examined the ethical responsibilities of reviewers and showed that confidentiality is directly linked to the functional integrity of the peer review process. When trust is broken, the basis on which authors accept the legitimacy of the process collapses with it. The default safe position is clear: confidential text is not uploaded to a third-party tool. This is not a technical prohibition; it is a question of ethical positioning.

Tennant and colleagues (2017), discussing the deep transformation of peer review systems, emphasised that confidentiality is not merely instrumental but structural — a load-bearing element of the trust that makes the system coherent. Extending that trust to AI tools is possible, but only through explicit journal policy, not through an individual reviewer's unilateral decision.

## 2. When Journal Policy is Absent

Major publishers and journals are actively developing policies on the use of AI in the review process. Elsevier, Springer Nature, Wiley, and many smaller publishers have issued guidance documents or added supplementary provisions to their ethics guidelines. But not every journal has an articulated position yet.

The absence of policy signals caution, not permission. If no explicit authorisation appears in the journal's reviewer guidelines, the reviewer does one of two things: asks the editor, or does not pass confidential text to any external tool. The second option can feel like forgoing a useful resource. But navigating a grey area now and confronting an integrity question later carries consequences that are considerably harder to manage.

COPE (2023), calling on publishers to develop clear policies on AI tools, specified that peer review deserved particular attention in that effort. WAME (2023) offered a parallel framework and drew attention to the risk of models becoming de facto collectors of confidential research data. Both organisations declined to treat ambiguity as permission. Asking the editor is both a practical resolution and a practice that strengthens the dialogue between reviewer and journal — and whatever the reply, the exchange itself constitutes a useful record.

## 3. Legitimate Uses

AI does not need to be excluded from peer review entirely. The boundary is drawn at the point where the manuscript's confidential content crosses into the tool. Within that boundary, a range of uses is both practical and ethically defensible.

A reviewer may use an AI tool to organise their own handwritten notes into a draft structure, or to generate the skeleton of a review report. Preparing a methodological checklist for a type of research design, querying a statistical approach at a general level, or refining the tone of a passage the reviewer has already written in their own words — all fall within the same category. What goes to the tool in these cases is the reviewer's own output: not raw quotations or paraphrases from the manuscript, but the reviewer's observations and questions.

Hosseini and colleagues (2023), examining AI use in scholarly publication, stressed that the distinction between a tool shaping a text's form and a tool generating its content must be preserved. That distinction applies directly here. A reviewer using AI to sharpen the expression of an observation they have already formed is engaged in editorial work. A reviewer feeding the manuscript into a model to have the evaluation performed for them is doing something categorically different.

The boundary, understood this way, is an enabling definition as much as a constraint. Using tools to make a review more organised, more clearly expressed, and more useful to the author and editor is possible. As long as the confidentiality line holds, that use remains ethically sound.

## 4. Illegitimate Uses

The uses that fall on the other side of the line are less ambiguous. Uploading the full text of a manuscript, its abstract, or specific sections to a third-party AI tool constitutes a confidentiality breach in the absence of explicit journal permission. The same applies to passing the author's hypotheses, unpublished findings, or novel methodological choices to a model.

Delegating the evaluation decision itself to the model is a distinct problem. Asking an AI tool whether a manuscript "should be accepted" or "how many points it deserves" attempts to substitute the model's output for the reviewer's academic judgment. This is not only a confidentiality concern; it is an accountability concern. A reviewer who accepts a review invitation takes on a responsibility. Offloading that responsibility to an anonymous output does not fulfil the obligation undertaken toward the journal.

In a related vein, constructing the entirety of a review report from model-generated text — submitted without substantive verification or independent judgment — turns the review from a scholarly evaluation into an AI exercise presented as one. The scientific assessment in a review report must originate from the reviewer's own conceptual framework. It need not avoid all external input, but it must ultimately reflect the reviewer's own reading, knowledge, and reasoning.

## 5. The Responsibility of Evaluation

A reviewer assesses an unpublished manuscript not only on technical grounds but on its contribution to the field, its conceptual coherence, the rigour of its methodology, the adequacy of its ethical safeguards, and the quality of its engagement with existing literature. That assessment draws on a body of accumulated knowledge: the field's open questions, its methodological disputes, its standards of evidence. It cannot be delegated to a system that lacks that knowledge in the relevant form.

An AI tool can produce a checklist. Asked whether a methods section contains common problems, a model may offer a useful list of considerations. But that list should be filtered through the reviewer's own judgment before any element enters the report. A concern raised by the model that the reviewer cannot substantiate is not a finding; it is an unverified suggestion.

Aczel and colleagues (2021) quantified the global labour cost of peer review and its effects on researcher time. In that context, it is understandable that AI assistance looks attractive. But reducing the burden of the work is not the same as transferring the responsibility for it. The distance between streamlining an evaluative task and surrendering the decision to another actor sits at the centre of what academic accountability means.

ICMJE (2024), developing guidance on AI in the scholarly publication workflow, specified that the reviewer's position as primary evaluator remains unchanged. Tools support the reviewer's work; they do not replace it. The evaluative judgment — including the final recommendation — belongs to the person who accepted the invitation.

## 6. Report Tone

A well-executed reviewer report does two things simultaneously: it names the weaknesses of a piece of work precisely, and it does so without a tone that turns the communication into something the author cannot act on. Harsh, dismissive, or opaque language does not strengthen scientific criticism; it raises questions about the reviewer's posture rather than the manuscript's quality.

AI can be genuinely useful here, operating strictly on the reviewer's own drafted text. A question like "does this sentence read as unnecessarily blunt, and how might it be reformulated more constructively?" can be directed at a passage the reviewer has already written. No confidential content enters the exchange. The result is tone maintenance: a form of editorial discipline that operates on the reviewer's output, not the manuscript's content.

What should not happen is using tone adjustment to soften the substance of a critique into vagueness. "There is a significant methodological problem in this section — the sample size is insufficient for this analysis" contains an assessable claim. If that sentence becomes "certain methodological considerations may warrant attention," the actual finding disappears. Tone work is not an exercise in evasion. Courtesy makes an honest observation navigable; it should not make an observation invisible.

Hames (2007) defined the function of a reviewer report as both an evaluative document and a developmental one. A report that fulfils both functions is specific enough to support a decision and constructive enough to help an author improve. Tone is the instrument that holds both functions in place.

## 7. Note to the Editor

Some observations a reviewer makes during evaluation are not appropriate to convey to the author through the standard report. When the data appears internally inconsistent in a way that raises questions beyond mere method, when a significant conflict of interest seems plausible, when the manuscript closely resembles a previously published work, or when ethical board approval is unclear, the reviewer communicates those concerns to the editor separately. This communication is kept apart from the author-facing report.

An AI tool can help format this note. But its content must come from the reviewer's own observation, not from a model's probabilistic assessment of whether the manuscript has an ethical problem. The concern being relayed to the editor rests on the reviewer's expertise and attention; it is not generated by asking a tool to scan for anomalies.

What the editor does with the information belongs to the editor. The reviewer fulfils their obligation by communicating clearly and specifically. A note that is precise, grounded in observable features of the manuscript, and free of speculative excess serves the editor best. Here, as elsewhere in the review workflow, the AI's contribution to tone and clarity must remain separate from its contribution to content.

## 8. Declaring AI Use

If AI was used during the review process, declaring that use is a natural part of an ethical posture. Some journals now ask this systematically through a dedicated field in the review submission form. Others have not yet created such a field. Where the field is absent, a brief note to the editor is sufficient.

The declaration does not diminish the reviewer's standing. On the contrary, it clarifies the scope of the use and makes the process transparent. A sentence along the lines of "I used an AI writing tool to review the tone of my own drafted notes; the manuscript text was not passed to the tool at any point" serves two purposes simultaneously. It signals honesty, and it documents that the confidentiality boundary was maintained.

COPE (2023) and WAME (2023) both recommend that declarations of this kind be encouraged at the policy level. In practice, that recommendation is being implemented unevenly across publishers and journals. A researcher who treats the policy gap not as licence but as an opportunity to exercise their own declaration initiative builds a consistent and defensible ethical record over time.

The presence of a declaration does not by itself validate the use that was made. But its absence — particularly when a journal later turns its attention to AI use in the review process — can place the reviewer in a difficult position that was unnecessary to create.

## 9. For Early-Career Researchers

Peer review is a learned academic practice. An experienced researcher knows how to name the strengths and weaknesses of a manuscript, how to engage with the literature on the author's behalf, and how to formulate criticism in a form that is useful rather than demoralising. That knowledge accumulates over time and cannot be shortcut.

An early-career researcher can use AI as a checking device rather than as a generator. Receiving a list of questions a model produces for a given research design and comparing it against one's own assessment is a legitimate learning exercise. Asking the model to write the review, or to determine the recommendation, is something different: it prevents the learning that peer review experience is meant to develop.

Supervisor oversight matters here in a way that goes beyond formal institutional requirements. The ethical habits a researcher forms during early review experiences become the baseline from which later conduct develops. Internalising the boundaries early — not as external rules but as understood reasons — is far preferable to forming patterns that have to be unlearned later.

Reading the journal's policy is itself a responsibility in this stage. Not as a formality to be processed and set aside, but as a genuine effort to understand what the relationship between reviewer and journal actually requires. That understanding shapes how a reviewer thinks inside the review process, not only how they behave at the submission step.

## 10. Skill Outputs

The `/peer-review-confidentiality-protocol` skill supporting this guide produces four concrete outputs for researchers who want to organise AI use during the review process within clear boundaries.

The first is a confidentiality verification protocol. When a review invitation is received, the skill provides a structured checklist for locating and interpreting the relevant journal's policy on AI tool use during review. If no applicable policy can be found, it appends a short question template the reviewer can send to the editor.

The second is a review report skeleton. The skill helps the reviewer organise the sections of their report starting from their own notes and observations. At no point in this process is confidential text processed; the tool works from the category headings and general observations the reviewer provides as input.

The third is a methodological question list. The skill offers sets of structured questions calibrated to common research design types in the social sciences. The list functions as a prompt for the reviewer's own reading: a reminder of which dimensions to consider before committing to an assessment.

The fourth is a draft AI use declaration. The skill provides a short, adaptable sentence set for notifying the editor of limited AI use during the review process. The red line here is the same as everywhere in this booklet: confidential manuscript text is not passed to a third-party tool without explicit policy permission from the journal.
