# Managing AI Style Traces in Revisions

The previous booklet built the rebuttal letter around a traceability matrix. When the response letter is finished, a revised manuscript remains on the desk. That manuscript has often been shaped, at some point in its development, with AI assistance. The assistance itself may be entirely legitimate. What it leaves behind is stylistic residue: recognizable rhetorical habits that settle over the author's own voice.

This booklet describes a two-layer method for managing that residue during revision. The purpose is not to conceal AI use. The purpose is to recover the author's own voice, their conceptual rhythm, and the register that belongs to their field. The governing principle is stated at the outset. Managing your style is an author's right. Disclosing the contribution is an author's obligation. These two levels are not interchangeable.

## 1. What a Trace Is, and What It Is Not

An AI trace is the aggregate of recurring stylistic patterns in model-assisted text. Among them: headings that announce in advance how many items will follow, paragraphs built to identical lengths, concepts arranged in mechanical groups of three, sentences that first declare what something is not before announcing what it is, and sections that close on the same summarizing cadence. None of these features is a defect in isolation. Stacked together, they erode the natural variation that human prose holds, and they do so regardless of how accurate the underlying content may be.

For a researcher writing across two languages, a second layer applies. When a draft moves from Turkish into English, or in the reverse direction, the model carries the source language's syntax and metaphors into the target. The result can be an academic interlanguage — technically readable but not fully natural in either tongue.

A trace is not a content error. A wrong finding, a fabricated reference, or an inflated claim each constitute an honesty problem, and other booklets in this guide address them directly. A trace operates at the level of style: it affects how a text sounds, not what it asserts. The question of deception is never resolved at the style level. It is resolved at the disclosure line, and this booklet returns to that line throughout.

## 2. What Detection Tools Can and Cannot Do

Attempts to determine from outside whether a text originated with a model have followed two tracks, and both have been put to empirical test. The first is human judgment. Else (2023) reported in *Nature* that ChatGPT-generated abstracts could fool experienced scientists. Gao and colleagues (2023) then placed the question inside a controlled design: blinded human reviewers correctly identified AI-generated abstracts at a meaningful rate, but in the same sessions they also misflagged a share of genuine abstracts as machine output.

The second track is automated detection. Weber-Wulff and colleagues (2023) systematically tested a range of detection tools and found that reliability was limited across the board. Accuracy fell sharply when the text had been paraphrased or had passed through machine translation — two conditions common in real revision workflows.

These findings converge on a single point. AI text detection is not reliable enough to carry the weight of scientific integrity. A mechanism that misses a significant portion of model-generated text while falsely accusing honest authors of the same cannot serve as a foundation for academic conduct. This booklet therefore rests on a disclosure regime, not a detection regime.

## 3. The Cost of a False Positive

A false positive is not an abstract statistical outcome. It is a false accusation directed at an author's honesty. That accusation does not fall equally on all authors. Liang and colleagues (2023) demonstrated that widely used GPT detectors produced disproportionately high false-positive rates on texts written by non-native speakers of English. Essays authored by speakers of other languages were misclassified as machine output at rates far exceeding those for native English writers.

The mechanism is straightforward. Most detectors measure the predictability of a text. A writer working carefully in a second language tends toward a narrower vocabulary and more regular sentence structure. This is not a deficit; it is the natural consequence of writing academically in a non-primary language. Detection tools, however, treat this regularity as a signal of machine origin.

A social scientist writing from Turkish into English sits precisely in this risk profile. An integrity regime built on detection places the bilingual researcher under suspicion from the outset. A regime built on disclosure extends trust to the author to state what was used, where, and to what end. The method in this booklet assumes the second regime.

## 4. Integrity Lives in Disclosure, Not in Invisibility

The sharpest test of the disclosure principle arises in this context: if I remove the AI trace from my text, am I hiding my AI use?

The answer depends on keeping two layers clearly apart. Hosseini, Rasmussen, and Resnik (2023), examining AI use in scholarly publication, emphasize that the distinction between a tool shaping the form of a text and a tool generating its content must be preserved. They locate the disclosure duty in the act of use, not in the visibility of the result in the final text. Style is the author's sovereign domain. A researcher may revise every sentence, dismantle every mechanical pattern, and rebuild the prose in their own register — none of this creates or cancels a disclosure obligation. The obligation attaches to what was used in the making.

When the trace is gone and the disclosure stands, the text is polished and the record is honest. When the trace is gone and the disclosure was removed with it, what has occurred is no longer style maintenance. It is an attempt to mislead the reader.

This distinction corresponds to the editorial standards this guide has followed throughout. Integrity bodies and editors' associations require that AI contributions be declared. None of them requires that a finished text retain the model's rhetorical habits. The sole requirement is transparent attribution of the contribution.

## 5. Layer One — Dismantling the Rhetorical Architecture

The first layer identifies recurring rhetorical patterns that are independent of language, and it begins with a count. Before any revision, the text is scanned and the occurrences of each pattern class are tallied. That count transforms the revision from an impressionistic exercise into a measurable one.

The pattern classes to scan for are those described in section one: headings and transitional sentences that announce the number of items to follow; mechanical runs of three; contrast frames held in place where no genuine contrast exists; paragraphs of interchangeable length; sections that close on the same cadence; transitions drawn from the same small set of connectors.

Dismantling proceeds class by class. An announcing heading is replaced by one that carries substantive content. Some triads are reduced to two items, others dissolved into prose. The contrast frame survives only where a real contrast warrants it. Paragraph length is returned to the weight of the content — a short point merits a short paragraph. Claude Code can support the scanning and counting in this layer; the decisions about what remains, what is removed, and which sentences align with the author's voice belong to the researcher. When the pass is complete, the count is taken again and the difference is recorded. That difference forms the first document in the defensibility file described in section nine.

## 6. Layer Two — Naturalness and the Hunt for Calques

The second layer addresses the bilingual writer specifically, and its target is a text that reads as native in the language it inhabits. A calque is a structure transferred word for word from one language into another. Model-assisted translation and re-authoring produce calques in quantity, because the model builds the shortest available bridge between source and target. A metaphor that sits naturally in English can read as strained in Turkish; a Turkish construction can lose its sense on the way into English.

The test for this layer is simple. A sentence is read aloud, and one question is asked: would a skilled academic writing natively in this language ever build this sentence? The ear catches what the eye has grown accustomed to. Borrowed syntax, transferred imagery, and imported academic boilerplate are heard before they are consciously identified.

In this guide's workflow, the test runs in both directions. Moving from a Turkish draft to an English re-authoring, the English is audited for naturalness. Returning from English-language literature notes to a Turkish section, the same audit is applied to the Turkish. Re-authoring is not translation. The argument is preserved; the sentences are rebuilt from within the target language. This is the most reliable corrective for the calque.

## 7. Homogenization and the Author's Voice

Noy and Zhang (2023) showed experimentally that generative AI assistance raised both speed and output quality in professional writing tasks, and that variance between writers narrowed in the process. Doshi and Hauser (2024) measured the counterpart effect at the level of content: writers given AI assistance produced individually more creative work, while the collection of outputs as a whole grew less diverse.

For scientific literature, these findings carry a warning. When thousands of researchers draw on the same model and leave its rhetorical patterns in place, the literature converges toward a single voice. Recovering one's own style is therefore not an aesthetic preference. It is a form of contribution to the intellectual diversity that a field requires in order to continue developing. An author who rebuilds their own sentences restores something to the reader and to the literature alike.

## 8. What Not to Do

The ethical limits of this method need to be explicit.

Removing the AI trace while retaining the disclosure is legitimate style maintenance. Removing the trace and the disclosure together is deception, and it falls outside what this method countenances. Running text through tools designed to defeat detection — while leaving the use undisclosed — is deception and additionally fragile, since detectors change. Denying AI use in response to a reviewer's direct question is a straightforward honesty violation. Treating trace removal as a substitute for citation verification is a category error.

The last point warrants separate attention. Walters and Wilder (2023) documented the prevalence of fabricated and erroneous citations in model-generated bibliographies. A fabricated reference is not a stylistic blemish; it is content fraud. No amount of prose revision addresses it. A text from which the AI trace has been removed, but whose references have not been independently verified, is a polished vehicle for risk. Citation verification is treated in this guide's booklet on APA 7 and DOI discipline.

| Situation | Assessment |
|---|---|
| Trace reduced, disclosure retained | Legitimate style maintenance |
| Trace reduced, disclosure removed | Misleading the reader |
| Text run through "humanizing" tools to defeat detection, use not disclosed | Deceptive and fragile practice |
| AI use denied in response to a reviewer's direct question | Clear honesty violation |
| Trace removal treated as a substitute for citation verification | Category error |

## 9. A Defensible Revision Workflow

Under real reviewer pressure, the method follows a fixed sequence. At the outset of revision, the disclosure statement is written down and left untouched for the duration. Then the pattern count is taken, layer one runs, and layer two follows with the read-aloud pass. The count is taken again and the change is recorded. The citation core is re-verified — covering references added during the revision as well as those that were already present. The final step is a deliberate check, by eye, that the disclosure statement still stands where it was placed.

What this sequence produces is a record that defends the process. When an editor or reviewer raises questions about AI use, the author holds a dated account: how many instances of each pattern class were found and how many were revised; which references were verified on which date against which source; the disclosure sentence, unchanged across versions. Claude Code can support the maintenance of this record. Defensibility is what can be shown when the question is asked.

## 10. Bridge — To the Moment the Process Breaks

Revision is the densest convergence of the workflows this guide describes. The archive, citation verification, bilingual re-authoring, the rebuttal letter, and the disclosure discipline all operate on the same text in the same week. The more moving parts a process has, the more ways it can stop moving.

The next booklet addresses exactly that moment. It lays out a troubleshooting protocol for advancing with method rather than with panic when something in the revision process breaks down. During a revision week, sound troubleshooting discipline is not merely a technical convenience — it is an academic necessity.
