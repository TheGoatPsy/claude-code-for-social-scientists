# Anti-AI-Trace Writing for Revisions

The previous booklet built the rebuttal letter around a traceability matrix, and its fourth section left one question deliberately open. When the response letter is done, there is also a revised manuscript on the desk, and that manuscript has usually been reshaped with AI assistance somewhere along the way. The assistance is legitimate. What lingers is stylistic residue: model-assisted prose carries recognizable rhetorical habits, and those habits sit on top of the author's voice until the text sounds like everyone and no one. This booklet describes a two-layer method for stripping that residue during revision, and it fixes one principle before the method begins. Governing your own style is an author's right. Disclosing the contribution is an author's debt. The two run on separate tracks, and neither can be traded for the other.

## 1. What a Trace Is, and What It Is Not

An AI trace is the sum of stylistic patterns that recur in model-assisted text. A heading that announces how many items will follow is one of them. So are paragraphs built to identical lengths, concepts marching in groups of three, sentences that first declare what something is not before announcing what it is, and sections that each close on the same summarizing cadence. None of these is a flaw on its own. Stacked together, they produce a symmetry that human prose does not naturally hold, and readers notice it even when they cannot name it.

For a writer working in two languages there is a second layer. When a draft crosses from Turkish to English or back, the model carries translation patterns with it: borrowed metaphors, syntax imported from the source language, sentences no one writing natively in the target language would ever build.

A trace is not a content error. A wrong finding, a fabricated reference, an inflated claim — these are honesty problems, and other booklets in this guide deal with them directly. A trace is a signature at the level of style. Removing it changes how the text sounds, not what it asserts. The question of deception is never settled at the level of style. It is settled at the disclosure line, and this booklet will keep returning to that line.

## 2. What Detection Tools Can and Cannot Do

Attempts to determine from the outside whether a text came from a model have run on two tracks, and both have been measured. The first is human judgment. Else (2023) reported in Nature that abstracts generated by ChatGPT could fool experienced scientists. Gao and colleagues (2023) then moved the question into a controlled design: blinded human reviewers correctly identified 68 percent of generated abstracts and, in the same session, wrongly flagged 14 percent of genuine abstracts as machine output. The second track is automated detection. Weber-Wulff and colleagues (2023) systematically tested fourteen detection tools, twelve publicly available and two commercial. Even the highest accuracy values stayed below 80 percent, and when AI-generated text was run through an AI paraphraser, overall accuracy collapsed to 26 percent. Their verdict is blunt: the available tools are neither accurate nor reliable.

These findings converge. A mechanism that misses a visible share of machine text while accusing a measurable share of honest authors cannot carry the weight of scientific integrity. Detection may have uses at the margins. As a foundation, it fails in both directions at once.

## 3. The Cost of a False Positive

A false positive is not an abstract statistic. It is an unfounded accusation aimed at an author's honesty, and it does not land on everyone equally. Liang and colleagues (2023) ran widely used GPT detectors over two corpora. On TOEFL essays written by non-native speakers of English, the average false-positive rate exceeded 61 percent, and nearly one essay in five was unanimously labeled machine output by every detector tested. The same detectors classified essays by US-born students accurately.

The mechanism works against the second-language writer by construction. Detectors measure perplexity: when a language model can easily predict the next word, perplexity is low and the text looks machine-made. A careful writer working in a second language naturally draws on a narrower vocabulary and builds more regular sentences. Writing correctly and cautiously becomes, under this regime, grounds for suspicion. Weber-Wulff and colleagues (2023) add a parallel finding from the other direction: false-positive risk rises sharply for machine-translated text.

A social scientist writing from Turkish into English stands in the middle of this picture. An integrity regime built on detection places the bilingual author under suspicion by default. A regime built on disclosure trusts the author to state what was used. The method in this booklet assumes the second regime, and the next section lays out its ethical floor.

## 4. Integrity Lives in Disclosure, Not Invisibility

Earlier booklets treated the disclosure principle in different contexts. Here it faces its sharpest test, in the form of a question every honest user of these tools eventually asks. If I remove the AI trace from my text, am I hiding my AI use?

The answer rests on keeping two layers apart. Hosseini, Rasmussen, and Resnik (2023), examining AI use in scholarly publication, insist that the distinction between a tool shaping the form of a text and a tool producing its content must not be allowed to blur, and they tie the duty to disclose to the use itself rather than to its visibility. Style is the author's sovereign territory. A researcher may recast every sentence, run any number of editing passes, and dismantle any pattern, and none of it creates or cancels a disclosure duty. The duty follows from what was used in the making, not from how the result reads. When the trace is gone and the disclosure stands, there is no deception: the text is polished and its record is honest. When the trace is gone and the disclosure went with it, what happened is no longer style maintenance. Its name is an attempt to mislead the reader.

This line matches the editorial standards this guide already follows. Publication ethics bodies and editors' associations require that AI contributions be declared. None of them requires that a text be left sounding like a model. The only requirement is that the contribution be stated plainly.

## 5. Layer One, Dismantling the Rhetorical Architecture

The first layer works on pattern classes that are independent of language, and it begins with counting. The text is scanned for each class, and the occurrences are tallied before anything is changed. That count is what makes the revision measurable rather than impressionistic.

The classes to scan for are familiar from section one. Headings and sentences that announce how many items are coming. Mechanical runs of three. The contrast frame kept up where no real contrast remains. Paragraphs of interchangeable length. Sections closing on the same cadence. Transitions drawn from the same small family of connectors.

Dismantling proceeds class by class. An announcing heading is replaced by one that carries content. Some triads drop to two items, some grow to four, some dissolve into the sentence. The contrast frame survives only where the contrast is real. Paragraph length is handed back to the content, since a short point deserves a short paragraph. In this layer Claude Code does the scanning and the counting, and the author decides what stays. When the pass is finished, the count is taken again and the difference is recorded. That difference becomes the first document in the defensibility file assembled in section nine.

## 6. Layer Two, Naturalness and the Hunt for Calques

The second layer belongs to the bilingual writer, and its target is a text that sounds native in the target language. A calque is a structure carried word for word from one language into another. Model-assisted translation and re-authoring produce calques in quantity, because the model builds the shortest available bridge between the two languages. A metaphor that sits naturally in English can stand awkwardly in Turkish, and a Turkish turn of phrase loses its meaning on the way into English.

The test for this layer is single and untechnical. Read the sentence aloud and ask one question. Would someone writing natively in this language ever build this sentence? The ear catches what the eye has grown used to. Borrowed academic boilerplate, imported syntax, and transferred imagery are heard before they are seen.

In this guide's workflow the direction runs both ways. Moving from a Turkish draft to an English re-authoring, the English is checked for naturalness. Returning from English-language literature notes to a Turkish section, the same check is applied to the Turkish. Re-authoring is not translation. Keeping the outline and rebuilding every sentence inside the target language is the most reliable antidote to the calque.

## 7. The Homogenization Problem and the Author's Voice

Why go to this trouble at all? The answer is not productivity. It is the diversity of voices a literature depends on. Noy and Zhang (2023) showed experimentally that generative AI assistance raises both speed and quality in professional writing tasks, and that inequality between writers shrinks as it does so. The distribution compresses toward the middle. Doshi and Hauser (2024) measured the counterpart at the level of content. Writers given AI assistance produced individually more creative stories, and the collection of stories as a whole grew less diverse.

For scientific literature these results read as a warning. When thousands of authors lean on the same model and leave its patterns standing, the literature drifts toward a single voice. Restoring your own style is therefore not cosmetic. It is a contribution to the polyphony a field needs in order to keep thinking. An author who rebuilds their own sentences rescues the reader, along with themselves, from the model's average.

## 8. What Not to Do

The boundary of the two-layer method is drawn by what it refuses to be.

| Situation | Assessment |
|---|---|
| Trace removed, disclosure kept | Legitimate style maintenance |
| Trace removed, disclosure dropped as well | Deception. Outside the method's purpose |
| Text run through "humanizer" tools to beat a detector, use left undisclosed | Deception, and fragile besides, since detectors change |
| AI use denied in answer to a reviewer's direct question | A direct honesty violation |
| Trace removal treated as a substitute for citation verification | A category error. Citation integrity is a separate process |

The last row deserves its own paragraph. Walters and Wilder (2023) documented how common fabricated and erroneous citations are in model-generated bibliographies. A fabricated reference is not a stylistic blemish. It is content fraud, and no amount of style work cleans it. A text with its trace removed and its references unverified is a polished carrier of risk. Citation verification itself is covered in this guide's booklet on APA 7 and DOI discipline.

## 9. A Revision Workflow You Can Defend

Under real reviewer pressure the method runs in a fixed order. Entering the revision, the disclosure statement is frozen. The sentence recording which parts of the text were produced with which assistance is written down and left untouched for the duration. Then the pattern count is taken, layer one runs, layer two closes with the read-aloud pass, and the count is taken again. The citation core is re-verified, covering the references that entered during the revision as well as the ones that left. The final step is a deliberate check, by eye, that the disclosure statement still stands where it stood.

What this order produces is a file that defends the process. If an editor or a reviewer asks, the author holds a dated record. How many instances of each pattern class were found, and how many were dismantled. Which reference was verified on which date against which database. The disclosure sentence, unchanged across versions. Claude Code automates the keeping of this record. Defensibility is whatever you can show when someone asks.

## 10. Bridge, to the Moment the Process Breaks

Revision is the densest intersection of the workflows this guide describes. The archive, citation verification, bilingual re-authoring, and disclosure discipline all work on the same text in the same week. The more moving parts, the more ways something can stop moving. A session may be cut off halfway. A file may turn up in a state no one left it in. The closing booklet is reserved for exactly that moment, and it lays out a troubleshooting protocol for moving forward with a method instead of panic.
