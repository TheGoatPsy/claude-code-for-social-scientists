# Grant Proposals and Project Texts, From Idea to Work Package

The distance between a research idea and a funding proposal is routinely underestimated. Writing an article means giving an account of work already done. Writing a proposal means making a convincing case for work not yet done — demonstrating, before a single observation has been recorded, that the work is feasible, necessary, and worth the funder's investment. That distinction reshapes the language, the argumentative structure, and the distribution of responsibility throughout the document. The difficulty a social scientist faces here is not only technical. It is the difficulty of making the future credible, of preserving intellectual freedom inside real constraints, and of meeting the varied expectations of a review panel within a single coherent text. AI assistance can genuinely help at every stage of this process. It can also introduce the most characteristic failure mode of grant writing at scale: overpromising on outcomes, inflating impact, and producing timelines that look plausible on paper but could not survive contact with fieldwork. This booklet works through each stage of the journey from idea to work package, identifying where AI earns its keep and where the researcher's own judgment is not negotiable.

## 1. The Difference Between a Research Idea and a Project Idea

A research idea is born from curiosity. The wish to explain a pattern, resolve a contradiction, or ask a question the literature has overlooked can sustain a researcher for years. That curiosity is, by its nature, unconstrained: it does not budget, does not schedule failure, and does not negotiate its scope with funding bodies. A project idea is the form that curiosity takes after systematic constraint has been applied. A fixed duration, a fixed budget, a specific call for proposals, and concrete deliverables. Not every good research idea makes a good project. A project is a research idea whose feasibility has been demonstrated.

The first move in that transformation is asking the feasibility question honestly. Can the question be answered in three years? Does the team genuinely have the methodological skills to carry this out? Will ethics committee review fit the projected timeline? Is field access genuinely secured? Locke, Spirduso, and Silverman (2014), in their comprehensive guide to dissertation and grant proposals, argue that these constraints must be placed on the table at the beginning of project planning. Left until the end, they produce proposals built on inflated premises — and experienced review panels sense that inflation in the document before they can articulate exactly where it comes from.

Claude Code is useful at two specific points in this transformation. It can generate a mapping draft that aligns the research question with the priority areas of the call for proposals. It can also produce an initial list of questions for breaking an idea down into concrete outputs and responsibilities. But the actual assessment of feasibility belongs to the researcher: the field conditions, team capacity, institutional support, and local constraints that no model has access to.

## 2. Problem, Aim, Objective, and Output

Four concepts recur in grant proposals and are most often conflated: problem, aim, objective, and output. Conflating them produces applications that are technically incoherent, and inconsistency among these four elements is among the most common grounds for rejection in peer review.

The problem states why the work is needed. A gap in the literature, a deficiency in policy or practice, the inadequacy of existing measurement instruments, conflicting findings in prior studies: these are all problem framings. The aim expresses the general direction of the project. It is orientational, not yet concrete. Objectives break the aim into measurable and time-bounded steps. A well-formed objective meets SMART criteria: it is specific, measurable, achievable, relevant, and time-limited. Outputs are the products that will actually be delivered by the end of the project — a dataset, a report, a publication, a policy brief, a training module. Every output links to at least one objective. No objective is left without an output.

A table that separates these four layers clearly serves two purposes simultaneously: it gives the applicant conceptual clarity and gives the review panel a tractable evaluation path. Claude Code can build this table from an operator's raw notes. But whether each objective is genuinely measurable and each output genuinely deliverable within the project window is for the researcher to verify. Przeworski and Salomon (1995) note that clarity and concision are at the centre of the reviewer persuasion process in research proposals. Elegance does not substitute for precision. Realism is the most durable form of persuasion.

## 3. The Logic of Work Packages

A work package is the executable unit of a project. Depending on the funding call, it may be labelled WP (Work Package) or Task. Every work package carries six essential components: the responsible person or institution, duration and schedule, task list, deliverables, risks, and dependencies. A well-written work package tells reviewers something that goes beyond conceptual sophistication: not only has this project been thought through, but its execution has been planned.

Two errors appear with opposite signs in work package writing. The first is reducing the work package to a restatement of the general aim — this conceals execution and erodes reviewer confidence. The second is filling the work package with excessive detail until the main argument becomes invisible. The right register is one in which a reader outside the project can understand who will do what in which month and which output that work serves, without needing to read the proposal twice.

AI can produce a work package draft. That draft is a useful starting point for deciding which tasks belong in which package. However, real capacity calculation — the number of working hours available to a research assistant per week, the realistic timeline for ethics board review, the scheduling of field access permits, and the rhythms of institutional administration — requires the researcher's local knowledge. Feed that knowledge in incorrectly, and the model will always produce an optimistic table. In large framework programmes such as Horizon Europe, where work packages must arrive with budget breakdowns, key performance indicators, and risk registers already attached, the draft Claude Code builds quickly is a scaffold, not a finished structure (European Commission, 2025).

## 4. The Timeline and the Logic of Gantt

A timeline is not an optimism document. It represents the real workload of a project and the dependencies between its components. Ethics committee approval, a pilot study, data collection, transcription, analysis, writing, internal review, revision, and final reporting — each of these has a genuine duration and must be thought through as such.

A Gantt chart arranges these components visually, showing tasks that can run in parallel on separate rows and tasks that depend on one another in sequential cells. In social science research, three durations are consistently underestimated. First, data collection: finding participants and securing their informed consent takes longer than anticipated, especially across multiple sites or with hard-to-reach populations. Second, transcription: one hour of recorded interview typically requires four or more hours of transcription time. Third, ethics committee review: in Turkish universities this can range from two weeks to four months depending on the institution, the type of study, and the committee's current workload. Any one of these three elements running late pushes everything on the critical path forward.

Claude Code can produce a Gantt draft. But the researcher supplies the real durations: the ethics committee turnaround at their own university, the genuine capacity of the team, any seasonal constraints in the field region, and the institutional calendar of holidays and administrative deadlines. The model cannot access these inputs, which means its output is always a draft requiring review — not a plan ready for submission.

## 5. Risk Management

Every project carries risk. A strong proposal does not pretend otherwise. It names the risks and presents a mitigation plan for each. Applications that write as if risk does not exist tend to confirm what Lamont (2009), observing grant review panels across disciplines, documented: experienced reviewers detect methodological weakness and unrealistic claims with a rapidity that often outstrips their ability to articulate exactly where the signal appeared. A credible risk analysis, on the other hand, signals that the researcher knows the field and has thought past the optimistic scenario.

In social science, risks cluster in several categories. Participant risk covers the possibility of failing to recruit the projected sample size and the possibility of higher-than-expected withdrawal rates. Data risk covers the possibility that a measurement instrument performs below expected psychometric standards and the possibility that field conditions change during the study. Team risk covers the departure of a research assistant midway through the project. Budget risk covers exchange rate volatility, unanticipated open access publication fees, and additional transcription costs. Regulatory risk covers ethics committee delays and institutional authorisation processes. For each risk, the proposal asks two questions: how can it be prevented in advance, and how will it be managed if it materialises?

Claude Code can generate a risk list and, working from the methods section, identify probable weak points. But local risks — the specific ethics committee practice at the researcher's institution, access and language barriers in the field region, the budget flexibility the host institution actually allows — belong to the researcher. Without that input, the risk table remains generic, and reviewers recognise it immediately.

## 6. The Impact Section

The impact section is the part of a grant proposal most vulnerable to inflation. Sentences such as "this study will contribute to the field" or "will inform policy decisions" appear in almost every application and for that reason carry no information for the reviewer. Strong impact text is concrete: it names who will do something differently, through which channel, and on what timescale.

In social science, impact flows through several distinct channels. Scientific impact means closing a gap in the literature, developing a new measurement instrument, or testing a theoretical framework that has previously lacked empirical grounding. Policy impact means specifying which institution or body could act on the findings, and what that action might look like. Practical or clinical impact means identifying how practitioners — clinicians, social workers, educators — might change what they do in direct response to the results. Societal impact means contributing to a more informed public on a matter of consequence. Each type of impact requires different evidence and addresses a different audience.

Secondary publication plans, conference presentations, policy briefs, and publicly accessible datasets are the concrete dissemination instruments that substantiate impact claims. A study may produce real-world change but, if the proposal does not show how that change will be enabled and tracked, the review panel has no basis on which to credit it. Claude Code can structure the impact section and frame each claim in the appropriate language for the funding body. But the answer to "who will do something differently because of this research" must come from the researcher's own understanding of the field. The model can frame the argument. It cannot supply the substance.

## 7. Budget Justification

A budget justification is not a financial list. It is a methodological argument. Every budget line must connect to a work package and to an output. The entry should not read "travel costs: €3,000" but rather "three site visits under Work Package 2, covering the Istanbul and Komotini regions, six days in total, accommodation and transport for one researcher, in support of data collection deliverable 2.3." That level of specificity serves two purposes: it helps reviewers assess whether the allocation is proportionate, and it creates a defensible record for the funder's audit process later.

The budget lines that are most difficult to justify fall into a small number of categories. Research assistant salaries must be calculated against the labour norms — minimum wage, academic pay scales, social security contributions — of the country where the work will be performed. Transcription and translation costs are frequently underestimated. Allocating for the actual hours rather than a round-number estimate signals methodological seriousness. Open access publication fees (article processing charges, or APCs) vary substantially by journal. SSCI and Scopus-indexed journals typically range from €1,000 to €3,500 per article, and a proposal that does not account for this risks a budget shortfall at exactly the moment when the research output is ready for dissemination. Software licences, data storage, ethics committee fees, and participant incentive payments each need a brief justification sentence.

For researchers working in Turkish institutions on internationally funded projects, a currency risk buffer deserves explicit treatment. Exchange rate volatility between the Turkish lira and euro or sterling can materially change the real cost of personnel and services midway through a project. The NIH application guide (2025) frames budget justification as integral to the proposal — not a financial appendix — and requires that each line directly serve the stated research objectives. The same principle applies in European and national social science funding calls.

Claude Code can produce the language for each budget line and combine the item description with its methodological rationale in a single sentence. The actual cost figures, however, are country-, institution-, and year-specific. Numbers the model generates are starting estimates, not confirmed amounts.

## 8. Ethics and Data Management Plan

In an AI-assisted project, the data management plan extends beyond the standard ethics section. Which data will be collected, where it will be stored, how it will be anonymised, which tools and models will see it, who will have access, and what will be shared or archived at the end of the project — each of these must be stated explicitly.

The data management plan has three layers. The first is the security of raw collected data: audio recordings, survey responses, observation notes. These must be held on secure servers in compliance with GDPR and, for researchers based in Türkiye, the Personal Data Protection Law (KVKK), with access restricted to project personnel. The second layer is the relationship between processed data and AI tools: sending raw transcripts to Claude Code or any similar model constitutes a data processing act and must be disclosed in the ethics protocol, with reference to the tool's data handling terms. The third layer is open data and reproducibility: which data types will be anonymised and uploaded to an open repository such as Zenodo or the Open Science Framework, and which will remain restricted on grounds of participant sensitivity.

The ethics approval process also encompasses the question of whether the funder itself requires an additional ethics review beyond the institutional committee. In Horizon Europe, this takes the form of a dedicated ethics self-assessment at the proposal stage and, if flagged, a full ethics review after selection. Claude Code can draft a data management plan structured around the FAIR principles — Findable, Accessible, Interoperable, Reusable — in the format required by most European funders. Decisions about which data to open, which tools to use, and which restrictions to apply rest with the researcher.

## 9. Pre-Submission Integrity Check

Before a proposal is submitted, an internal consistency audit should be run. Five questions form the core of this audit. Does every research question have at least one corresponding work package? Does every work package have a budget line? Does every output have both a budget allocation and a timeline slot? Does every impact claim have at least one concrete dissemination activity behind it? Does the ethics section cover every type of data that will be collected?

When all five questions return a yes, the proposal is structurally complete. When any link is missing, whatever polish the AI has applied to the prose is concealing a structural gap — and review panels find these gaps systematically. Friedland and Folt (2009), writing on the standards of evidence required in scientific proposals, observe that every methodological step must answer the questions who, what, where, when, and how before a reviewer can treat it as credible. That standard holds for social science proposals as directly as it does for natural science ones.

You can delegate the integrity check to Claude Code. The model will read the draft and list the missing links. But every item on that list must be verified by the researcher. The model can misread a vague sentence in the proposal as evidence of a genuine connection. The researcher's final reading cannot be delegated.

## 10. Skill Outputs

The `/grant-proposal-workpackage-builder` skill covers all the components addressed in this booklet. It produces an aim-and-objective distinction table, a work package template carrying the responsible party, timeline, tasks, deliverables, risks, and mitigation strategies, a Gantt draft, a risk and mitigation register, an impact statement scaffold, a budget justification template, and a data management plan summary.

The primary value of the skill is structural: it forces the researcher to think through each component separately rather than merging them in a single undifferentiated narrative. A blank template makes visible what is missing. A filled template surfaces internal inconsistencies. Hosseini, Rasmussen, and Resnik (2023) connect the disclosure of AI contribution directly to its use, and that principle applies here: if the skill has contributed substantially to the structure of the proposal, and the funder's system provides a field for it, the disclosure belongs there. Disclosure does not weaken the application. In the eyes of a careful reviewer, it signals the author's methodological transparency.

The skill output is a draft, not a submission-ready document. The finished proposal always passes through the researcher's local knowledge, institutional reality, and understanding of the specific review panel's expectations before it goes out. As Belcher (2019) shows in laying out a week-by-week publication timetable, starting each component early and treating each section as a distinct working session is the practice that prevents last-minute structural collapse. The same logic applies to grant writing: the proposal built in stages, with each component pressure-tested before the next is added, is the one that holds up under review.
