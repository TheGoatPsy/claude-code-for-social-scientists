# Authorship and Contribution Traces, Who Did What, Where It Changed

A journal article's title page shows a line of names. Behind that line is something else: the literature search, the data cleaning, the negotiation of field access, the ethics board correspondence, the clinical interpretation, the analysis, the first draft, the revision, the language correction, the response to reviewers. Who did which part of this, when it changed, which decision was made by whom and on what grounds. None of it appears on the title page. But all of that invisibility — sooner or later, in contribution disputes, in journal submission forms, in evaluation processes — has to become visible all at once.

The core claim of this booklet is this. Shared authorship is not a courtesy to resolve before the manuscript is submitted. It is a responsibility agreement established at the beginning of a project, updated as contributions evolve, and audited at submission. And it is never merely a technical file arrangement. At its core it is ethical.

## 1. Why shared authorship is an ethical matter

Academic authorship is an act by which a person declares that they have genuinely contributed to a portion of the scientific content and accepts a share of responsibility for its accuracy. This definition matters, because adding a name to a manuscript and accepting responsibility for its content are two different things. One is visibility; the other is accountability.

The International Committee of Medical Journal Editors (2024) defines four criteria for authorship: a substantive contribution to the conception or data of the work; drafting or critically revising the manuscript; approving the final version to be published; and agreeing to be accountable for any part of the work. Contributions that do not meet all four criteria belong in the acknowledgements section, not on the author line. That boundary is not always easy to draw. But when it is left undrawn, the result is most often either names that were not earned, or genuine labor that goes unrecognized.

Both are defined as ethical problems. Gift authorship places the name of someone who made no real contribution — a supervisor in a position of power, an institutional administrator, a grant holder — on a paper. Ghost authorship is the mirror: the person who did the actual writing remains nameless. Resnik and colleagues (2016) have documented that a significant proportion of scientific journals lack explicit authorship policies capable of distinguishing the two. That gap does not arise from the absence of rules so much as from deliberate ambiguity.

The source of the ambiguity is often good intention. Everyone contributes, but no one writes down how contributions will be counted. As the project progresses, expectations diverge. When no record has been kept, resolving a dispute becomes a competition between memories rather than a problem of establishing fact. A contribution ledger ends that competition before it begins.

## 2. CRediT roles and the social sciences

The Contributor Roles Taxonomy, CRediT for short, is a framework developed to define contributions in a shared vocabulary. Brand and colleagues (2015) showed that this framework cuts to the heart of the authorship problem: authorship is not a single act but a composite of up to fourteen distinct contribution roles. Conceptualization, methodology, software, validation, formal analysis, investigation, resources, data curation, writing — original draft — writing — review and editing — visualization, supervision, project administration, and funding acquisition.

Allen and colleagues (2014) articulate why this disaggregation is necessary: when it is not visible who did what, it becomes impossible to both reward and assign responsibility for the contribution. CRediT was designed to provide that visibility.

For the social science researcher, these roles cannot be applied mechanically. A clinical psychology study carries questions of its own: who secured field access, who managed participant relationships, who provided local-language mediation, who handled ethics board coordination. These may not map cleanly onto CRediT categories. They should enter the contribution ledger regardless. The taxonomy provides a starting vocabulary. The researcher adapts that vocabulary to the reality of their field.

Holcombe (2019) argues that this adaptation is part of a broader reform of the authorship system. Authorship should not be an umbrella that conceals contribution but a whole that makes each component visible. In the social sciences this argument carries particular weight, because projects are so often built precisely on "invisible" contributions — field connections, community trust, cultural mediation — that current authorship systems make structurally difficult to name.

## 3. The author-order conversation

In most social science journals, author order carries hierarchical meaning. The first author is the primary executor and intellectual owner of the work. The last author most often signals the senior supervisor or project overseer. The order among those in between is read differently depending on context and discipline.

The field is quite clear on when this order should be discussed: at the beginning of the project. McNutt and colleagues (2018) show that the large majority of authorship disputes are the product of a conversation that was never had at the project's outset. An early conversation does not issue a final verdict. As contributions change, the order can be renegotiated. But an order that was never discussed has no capacity to produce justice in a crisis.

The supervisor-student relationship makes this conversation more delicate. Power asymmetry directly affects the student's ability to articulate expectations. For this reason, the responsibility for initiating the author-order discussion should rest with the supervisor. Making the student's contribution visible is not merely a matter of fairness; it is a pedagogical obligation. A first conversation conducted with explicit intentionality changes the ground on which every subsequent disagreement takes place.

The author-order note itself can be brief: the working assumption adopted at the outset, the criteria used to make that determination, and how a change would be discussed if contributions shifted. Three items, one page, produced at the beginning of the project.

## 4. The contribution ledger

The contribution ledger is a living record of who did what throughout a project. It requires no elaborate system. What is sufficient: name, date, work performed, file or section affected.

Kept that lean, a ledger survives. Long forms go unfilled and therefore accomplish nothing. Monthly meeting minutes get forgotten when the project falls behind schedule. A brief entry made within two minutes of each contribution becomes a system precisely because it is not a burden.

AI assistance belongs in this record. Which model was used and at which stage — first draft, editing, literature summary, data-analysis support, response-letter drafting. Who carried out the human review and how extensively they intervened. Which portions of the output were accepted, which were modified, which were entirely rewritten. This distinction does not diminish the authors' contribution. It clarifies, for each author, what their contribution actually was.

COPE (2023) is explicit: AI tools cannot meet authorship criteria. A model's output can be raw material. Responsibility is always human. The contribution ledger holds the trace of that responsibility: who reviewed what, which decision was made on which grounds, what the model proposed and what the human accepted or rejected. This trace has value both for individual authors and for a scientific record that can be held to account.

## 5. File versioning and change traces

In shared authorship, file versioning carries meaning beyond practical organization. It is a memory of how the text evolved — which change was made by whom and when, which decision was reached at which stage of the work.

More than one tool can be used together to hold this memory. Track changes keeps every comment and correction signed and dated. Git, particularly for text files, shows the difference between versions line by line, records who changed what and when, and retains that record permanently. A file-naming convention offers a simpler but reliable alternative: `manuscript_v1_OI_20260510.docx` preserves who edited which version, with a timestamp. The revision history, for its part, is the documentary record of the author-journal correspondence across submission rounds.

What matters is not finding the single correct tool. What matters is that the answer to three questions remains traceable: who made a change, why they made it, and who approved it. When those three things persist as a record, it becomes possible to return to reviewer comments during revision, reconstruct facts in a dispute, and verify the contribution statement at submission.

The qualitative components of social science research make this record especially valuable. How an interview transcript was interpreted, at which step a thematic analysis was structured, how coding decisions were negotiated. These processes do not generate automatic traces the way numerical data does. Whether a trace is kept is the researcher's decision. Making that decision at the start of the project is a far more stable foundation than trying to reconstruct it under pressure months later.

## 6. Separating the AI contribution

Every point at which an AI tool enters the research process should generate a separate entry in the contribution record. Each step at which the model rewrote a passage, produced a literature summary, suggested code for data analysis, or drafted a response letter should be noted alongside the human oversight that was applied.

This separation does not diminish the authors' contribution. It clarifies which decisions belong to them. The scientific responsibility for the claim in a paragraph that the model rewrote rests with the authors. Who evaluated the theoretical justification for a framework the model proposed? Who rejected it, who accepted it, and how was it adapted? These decisions carry the intellectual spine of the work.

COPE (2023) uses the metaphor of a musical instrument: whoever plays the instrument is responsible for the output. This metaphor clarifies authorship responsibility, but it also implies that the playing must be recorded. Which tool, at which stage, for which purpose. Who reviewed the output. How it was verified. This information is necessary both for the ethical disclosure statement and for the methodological transparency that allows someone else to follow the same process one day.

Journal submission forms are asking increasingly specific questions: Was AI used? For what purpose? In which section? How was human oversight applied? These questions are hard to answer without preparation. A trace held in a contribution ledger makes them answerable in minutes.

## 7. Shared responsibility through peer review

Peer review is often the most intellectually demanding phase of a manuscript's life. The critiques arrive: the method is weak, the sample is too small, the theoretical framing is inconsistent, the discussion is underdeveloped. The responses to those critiques determine whether the manuscript is published.

In shared authorship, who will respond to what should have been considered well in advance. The roles established in the contribution ledger activate here. The author with primary responsibility for methodology addresses methodological critiques. The contributor with clinical expertise handles clinical interpretation questions. The person who ran the analysis answers data analysis queries. Having one person write all the responses may be practical, but each response should be read, approved, and where possible signed off by the relevant contributor.

This distribution is not only a matter of fairness. It is a matter of quality. When a reviewer questions a method with precision, the most accurate answer comes from the person who designed it. The difference between someone else producing a "reasonable" response and that person providing the real one can determine whether the reviewer initiates a second round of major revisions.

McNutt and colleagues (2018) advocate a model that extends authorship accountability across the full revision cycle. The commitment made at submission holds for errors in the final text the journal publishes. This understanding of responsibility explains why revision should be a distributed and traceable process from the start.

## 8. Conflict and delay

Shared authorship is not linear. Someone does not deliver on time. Someone objects to the direction the project has taken. Someone feels their name appears too infrequently in the contribution ledger. Someone objects, after the fact, to the use of an AI tool. These are the normal tensions of academic life. The contribution ledger was not designed for these tensions. But when they arise, it functions as a document.

In conflict, the record substitutes for memory. What was written down is what gets discussed — not what anyone claims to have said. This distinction is decisive. "I said this" and "the record shows this" are different claims. The second moves a dispute from a conflict between personalities onto objective ground.

In delay, the record serves a different function. When it is visible which task has been completed, which is outstanding, and what the reason for the delay is, internal pressure within the group becomes more productive. The person who has not delivered faces not a vague accusation but a specific gap. That distinction makes both resolution and preservation of the relationship easier.

Resnik and colleagues (2016) show that in journals whose authorship policies do not anticipate these situations, dispute resolution runs almost entirely through informal channels. When institutional mechanisms are eventually engaged, the damage is usually already done. A contribution ledger does not close that gap entirely, but knowing a record exists raises the threshold at which a dispute escalates noticeably.

## 9. The pre-submission contribution statement

Before the manuscript is submitted, the contribution table should be read one final time, confirmed by each author, and formatted to the journal's requirements. This last reading is not a bureaucratic step. It is the final checkpoint that preserves both the scientific and relational integrity of the project.

The checklist has five components. Is the author order consistent with the agreement reached at the project's beginning, or was it updated as contributions changed? Do the CRediT roles reflect actual contribution, or are they a copy of titles? Are conflict-of-interest disclosures complete for every author? Is the AI contribution — which tool, in which section, under what human oversight — declared openly? Does the acknowledgements section include everyone who made a genuine contribution but did not meet authorship criteria: funders, field coordinators, copy editors, data managers?

This statement should be understood not as a formality required by the journal but as the public registration of every collaborator's work. Holcombe (2019) observes that under increasing pressure for transparency, the contribution statement is no longer merely a formality; it is becoming part of the methodological credibility of the work itself. Readers are no longer asking only whether the findings are reliable. They are also asking who produced them.

## 10. Skill outputs

The `/authorship-contribution-ledger` skill produces the following outputs: a CRediT role table, a project-opening author-order note, a contribution ledger template, an AI contribution separation record, a pre-submission contribution statement checklist, and a revision responsibility map.

Each output targets a specific blind spot in the lifecycle of shared authorship. The CRediT table draws the contribution boundaries that were unclear at the project's start. The ledger records how those boundaries shifted. The AI contribution record documents human responsibility for every step at which a model was involved. The revision map makes explicit who is accountable for what during peer review. The submission statement ties all of it to the journal's formal requirements.

The skill makes these processes technically convenient. But the value of any convenience depends on what it makes easier. What this one makes easier is moving shared authorship out of the register of courtesy and into a system of auditable academic accountability. That move serves both the authors themselves and the integrity of the scientific record.
