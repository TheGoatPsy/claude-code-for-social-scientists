# Multilingual Concept Validity, Thinking Academically Between Turkish, English, and Greek

The quietest problem facing a multilingual researcher is often the one that looks most technical. Translation is not enough. The question is whether a concept carries not only its form but its weight across languages. This booklet addresses how a researcher thinking academically between Turkish, English, and Greek conducts that verification. The core claim is this: in the social sciences, language is not a transmission channel. It is thought itself. A concept moving between languages may preserve its shape while losing its content. Making that loss visible is part of the researcher's methodological responsibility.

## 1. The difference between translation and concept equivalence

Translation and concept equivalence are two distinct operations that are frequently conflated in language work. Translation seeks the grammatical and semantic counterpart of a text in another language. Concept equivalence asks a narrower and more demanding question: can this idea live in the target language with the same theoretical weight and the same cultural context?

A text may be fluently translated into English. The sentences may be well-formed, the punctuation correct, the academic register consistent. But if concepts such as anxiety, distress, belonging, minority, or community have drifted according to context, the text has become formally successful and conceptually unreliable. What creates kinship between two versions of a text is not the surface matching of words but whether concepts carry the same claim. Temple and Young (2004), examining translation processes in qualitative research, place this distinction at the center of their analysis: they demonstrate that translation is not merely a grammatical practice but an interpretive one that directly shapes the researcher's conceptual stance. Van Nes and colleagues (2010) document how meaning loss accumulates silently when translation is conducted in qualitative research contexts.

The researcher's task is to distinguish these two operations. Fluent translation is not a guarantee of concept equivalence. In fact, fluent translation is often what renders conceptual drift most invisible: once a reader can understand the text, the questioning stops.

## 2. The cultural load of a concept

Concepts do not live like dictionary entries. Every concept has an academic history, a clinical practice, an institutional language, and a social context. These contexts do not map cleanly across languages. Wierzbicka (1997) has shown that key words in different languages carry culture-specific semantic structures. The English word mind has no direct equivalent in Turkish. The German Heimat becomes homeless in other languages. The Greek word λύπη (lype) is not simply sadness but a particular layer of melancholy and collective mourning.

A similar tension exists between the Turkish kaygı and the clinical English anxiety. In Turkish, kaygı can cover the range of everyday worry, the restlessness felt before an examination, and existential unease, all under one word. Clinical anxiety in English refers to a specific cluster of disorders, a pattern of symptoms affecting functioning, a category defined within the DSM or ICD framework. When a scale is translated into Turkish and these two semantic layers are conflated, what the scale measures changes. The data has not changed but the meaning transported by the data has drifted to a different location.

The problem of cultural load is not confined to psychological concepts. Sociological, historical, and legal concepts carry similar tensions. This is why a multilingual researcher, when moving core concepts from one language to another, must ask at every step: does the target-language equivalent carry the same specificity and the same scope as the source-language term?

## 3. Clinical psychology concepts

Clinical psychology is one of the fields most sensitive to concept validity, because its concepts are tied both to measurement instruments and to clinical diagnostic frameworks. A mistranslated concept is not merely a semantic error; it is a psychometric reliability problem.

Terms such as distress, impairment, symptom, functioning, resilience, and coping each carry specific technical content in clinical contexts. For distress in Turkish, a researcher might choose sıkıntı, psikolojik zorlanma, or ıstırap. This choice is not arbitrary. Sıkıntı covers a broad everyday semantic range and weakens the clinical load. Psikolojik zorlanma is a technical equivalent but may strike readers as unfamiliar if deployed without explicit framing. Keder evokes a form of sorrow associated primarily with emotional loss and does not cover the full range of distress.

Vandenberg and Lance (2000), in their comprehensive review of measurement invariance testing, demonstrate that whether a scale measures the same conceptual structure consistently across cultural or linguistic groups must be tested systematically rather than assumed. Beaton and colleagues (2000) define a five-stage protocol for the cross-cultural adaptation of self-report measures: translation, synthesis, back-translation, expert committee review, and pre-test. Each stage of this protocol is a conceptual checkpoint.

The researcher should construct a concept passport to manage this process. For each major clinical concept, the source-language term, target-language equivalents, the contexts in which each equivalent is appropriate, and the contexts in which it is not should all be recorded. This record preserves conceptual coherence as the manuscript grows.

## 4. Context in social science concepts

Social science concepts may appear less technical than clinical ones. That appearance is misleading. Terms such as community, identity, belonging, minority, integration, and marginalization carry historical, legal, and social context. That context does not transfer directly across languages.

Consider the concept of minority. In the context of Western Thrace, minority is not a demographic description. It is a political and historical position defined by the Treaty of Lausanne, covered by specific guarantees in international law, encompassing rights to education, worship, and cultural expression. When a researcher writes "Muslim minority in Western Thrace" in an English text, the decision about how to render this in Turkish — as Batı Trakya Müslüman azınlığı, as Batı Trakya Türk Azınlığı, or as a treaty-based formulation derived from the treaty's own language — is a methodological one. Each choice draws a different political and historical frame.

Yildiz (2012), examining multilingual experience beyond the monolingual norm, shows how language choice shapes identity and positioning. This framework is particularly important for researchers working in minority contexts. A multilingual researcher must be aware of how the social position they occupy is constructed through language, and how that construction seeps into the scientific text.

Integration and marginalization require similar care. In sociological literature, does integration denote assimilation into the dominant group, the preservation of cultural difference, or a process of mutual recognition? The answer varies by model, and the Turkish entegrasyon and the English integration do not always call up the same model. Before a concept enters a text, the theoretical framework being adopted should be stated, and both the source and target versions of the text must draw from that same framework.

## 5. The concept passport

The concept passport is a methodological tool for maintaining conceptual consistency across a multilingual research text. Its function is straightforward: for each major concept, a single table records the source-language term, target-language equivalents, the contexts in which each equivalent is used, the contexts in which it is avoided, and example sentences.

This table is not a mechanical glossary. It also records where a concept is contested, where more than one equivalent is defensible, and what the author's explicit preference is. In this way, as the text grows or as the writer moves from one chapter to the next, the same concept is not rendered with different words that silently shift its meaning.

A productive method for building the passport is to test each major concept in the text against four questions. What is the common target-language equivalent of this concept? Does that equivalent fully carry the technical content of the source-language term? In which contexts does that equivalent fall short? Are there alternative equivalents, and what distinguishes them? The answers to these questions constitute the passport.

The /multilingual-concept-validity-audit skill supports this process. It extracts key concepts from a text, lists source and target equivalents for each, flags points at risk of contextual drift, generates specific warnings for local or culturally particular concepts, and produces a list of decisions that require author approval. The decision itself always belongs to the researcher; these are methodological and theoretical choices, not language problems to be solved automatically.

## 6. Greek and local context

Greek academic and institutional terminology requires a distinct layer of attention for social scientists working in a Greek context or conducting research in a multilingual social field such as Western Thrace. In this setting, Greek is not only the data-collection language. The institutional reality itself is constructed in Greek.

Place names are the first test of this attention. Official Greek names should be used for cities such as Komotini, Xanthi, and Alexandroupoli. In Turkish academic texts, Komotini rather than Gümülcine is preferred, because this is not merely a matter of geographic naming: it is a matter of consistent identifiability within the international scholarly record. Similarly, a village name such as Mischo should be preserved in the form in which it appears in local usage and historical sources.

Institutional names require the same care. If a Greek university, state institution, or civil society organization enters the text, the original Greek name should be given in parentheses and followed by a Turkish or English rendering where needed. This practice gives readers a means of verification and increases the traceability of the text.

Legal concepts demand separate attention. A term originating in Greek law, when translated into Turkish or English academic prose, must preserve its legal content. An approximate translation of a legal concept introduces interpretive error into any text requiring legal analysis. Amano and colleagues (2023) and Ramírez-Castañeda (2020) document empirically that researchers thinking in a language other than English face not only a language barrier but a conceptual representation problem. That problem takes its sharpest form in local contexts where the relevant concepts are embedded in specific legal, historical, and social structures.

## 7. Where machine translation thins the argument

Machine translation has improved substantially in recent years. For everyday texts and even technical documents, it produces usable output. But when academic concept validity is at stake, machine translation fails at specific and systematic points.

The first is the absence of context sensitivity. Machine translation maps a concept to its most statistically probable equivalent. But in an academic text, distress may require psikolojik zorlanma rather than sıkıntı. The system does not capture this contextual difference because context here requires holistic interpretation: it requires the researcher's theoretical commitment.

The second is the tendency to flatten cultural semantic layers. Social exclusion becomes dışlanma, which looks sufficient. But structural exclusion, institutional exclusion, and interpersonal exclusion are distinct concepts, both theoretically and in terms of measurement. Brislin (1970), examining back-translation methodology, draws attention to the importance of testing translational equivalence not only at the lexical level but at the semantic and conceptual level. Machine translation output is not a tool for that test; it is itself what must be tested.

The third is that machine translation is coherent at the sentence level but loses the thread across an extended argument. A system can handle a sentence adequately while being unable to verify whether an argument spanning several paragraphs preserves the same conceptual frame throughout. For this reason, machine translation output should be treated as a raw draft, not as a text whose concept validity has been established.

## 8. Writing in English without losing Turkish

Producing English academic texts for international publication is increasingly a structural requirement. Amano and colleagues (2023) have shown that non-native English-speaking researchers face significant disadvantages across the publication and peer review process. That reality cannot be wished away. But the only available response is not to surrender Turkish thought to English.

When a concept or finding that originates in a Turkish context is moved into an English text, two things require attention. The first is making the concept's origin visible. A researcher studying minority experiences in Turkey draws on concepts shaped by the Turkish academic tradition and social history. Explaining that origin is part of the text's intellectual honesty. The second is ensuring that the English text does not diminish the Turkish text. Researchers sometimes simplify local concepts or absorb them into general terms in order to meet the expectations of an international readership. This approach produces conceptual loss and severs the research from its Turkish-language grounding.

Yildiz (2012), examining how the monolingual norm is produced and how multilingual life disrupts it, implies that the multilingual researcher can treat this disruption not as a weakness but as an analytic resource. Thinking across Turkish, English, and Greek is not only a practical difficulty. It is also the capacity to see the limits and the gaps that each language's conceptual system produces. That capacity is what makes the research distinctive.

## 9. Audit questions

The conceptual audit of a multilingual text should be conducted during the writing process, not after the text is complete. Conceptual drift most often occurs not between sections but within a single sentence. The following questions provide a framework for this audit.

Do the core concepts carry the same claim in both languages? Has a concept used in the Turkish text drifted to a different semantic field in the English text? Have clinical terms fallen into everyday language, or is their technical content preserved? Can local concepts — particularly legal and historical ones — maintain their context in the target language? Is the English text re-interpreting the Turkish text, or is it constructing the same argument on a different linguistic surface? Do the measurement concepts used in methodological sections imply the same measurement model in both languages?

The answers to these questions are recorded in the concept passport and re-checked at each revision stage. Amano and colleagues (2023) and van Nes and colleagues (2010) demonstrate that language differences directly affect data quality in both qualitative and quantitative research. The audit is the most practical means of reducing that effect.

Vandenberg and Lance (2000) and Beaton and colleagues (2000) show, in quantitative contexts, that the cross-cultural validity of measurement instruments must be secured through systematic testing rather than assumed. Those testing procedures can be adapted as conceptual audit questions for qualitative research.

## 10. Skill outputs

The /multilingual-concept-validity-audit skill provides conceptual audit support for bilingual and trilingual research texts. It extracts key concepts from a manuscript, lists source and target equivalents for each, flags points at risk of contextual drift, produces specific warnings for locally embedded or culturally particular concepts, and generates a list of decisions requiring author approval.

The output is organized as a table: the source-language term in the left column, target-language equivalents and context notes in the middle columns, and approval status in the right column. This table can serve as the first draft of the concept passport.

The skill also supports back-translation steps. In the method systematized by Brislin (1970), a text that has been translated into English is translated back into the source language by an independent translator, and the two Turkish versions are compared to identify conceptual drift. The researcher can use the skill output to structure that comparison.

This skill moves bilingual work beyond language parity and into the domain of conceptual responsibility. The reliability of research depends not only on the quality of the data but on the validity of the concepts that carry it.
