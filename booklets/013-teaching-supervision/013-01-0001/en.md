# AI in Teaching, Course Design, Supervision, and Student Feedback

This booklet addresses the social scientist not only as researcher but as instructor, thesis advisor, and clinical supervisor. The source discipline and ethical grounding established in research take on different but equally critical forms in teaching: protecting the student's labor, keeping learning responsibility in the right place, and sustaining the fairness of assessment.

AI can take on specific functions across all these roles. It can draft a course syllabus, accelerate reading list suggestions, frame a rubric, and refine the language of feedback. What it cannot do is take over the student's thinking process. It cannot displace the real gain of an assignment — the experience through which a student learns to construct an argument, engage with a source, and draw a critical inference. It cannot process clinical supervision material without prior anonymization. It cannot establish a fair evaluative ground in relation to the student.

Defining these limits from the outset is not a rejection of AI. It is knowing where the tool is useful, where it must stand aside, and where pedagogical judgment must remain separate from anything the tool can produce.

## 1. The difference between teaching and research

In research, the aim is to produce knowledge: to establish a finding, test a concept, document a relationship. In teaching, the aim is different — to develop the student's capacity for thought, to ensure that the student engages with ideas independently, to build an environment in which the student internalizes the process of producing knowledge rather than simply consuming it.

This distinction directly shapes the role AI can play in each context. When a researcher uses a tool for their own work, they bear sole responsibility for the accuracy and integrity of the product. When an instructor brings AI into a course, accountability becomes layered: the instructor is responsible both for their own scholarly judgment and for the student's learning process.

Zawacki-Richter and colleagues (2019), in their systematic review of AI applications in higher education, draw attention to the gap between technical capacity and pedagogical design. The existence of a tool does not automatically justify integrating it into the classroom or into assignment assessment. What pedagogical purpose it serves, which learning objective it secures, and how it affects assessment fairness must each be defined explicitly.

The purpose of an assignment is not merely to receive a product. The process through which the student produces that product — the experience of constructing the argument, encountering the source, making and correcting errors — is the point of the exercise. A use of AI that bypasses this process does not help the student learn; it obstructs learning. Zimmerman (2002), in his foundational work on self-regulated learning, makes this precise: independent thinking capacity develops through active engagement in the production process, not through the product alone.

## 2. Syllabus design

A course syllabus is, at the most surface reading, a weekly topic list. Its function runs considerably deeper. It is a learning contract with the student: a document specifying what to expect from the course, which competencies will be developed, how performance will be assessed, and what the instructor's pedagogical priorities are.

AI can serve a drafting function in this process. If you describe a course's purpose, target audience, and number of weeks to Claude Code, it generates a reasonable skeleton: a suggested weekly flow, possible reading categories, a distribution of assessment components. This draft removes the first obstacle of beginning from nothing and can help the instructor shift attention from logistics to content.

But the pedagogical backbone of a syllabus belongs to the instructor and cannot be delegated. Why does this reading appear in this particular week? What sequencing best supports the student's conceptual development? Which weeks will be demanding and how will that difficulty be met? Which assignment targets which thinking skill? These are not questions a generative model is unable to answer, but answering them requires knowing the course, the student cohort, and the institutional context as well as the instructor knows them — and that is not possible.

Kasneci and colleagues (2023), in their extensive review of opportunities and challenges for large language models in education, find that models can be effective at content generation while emphasizing that pedagogical appropriateness decisions must remain with the instructor. A syllabus, once drafted, is a living document requiring constant revision. The instructor who observes where the class has arrived at midterm adjusts the weight of the following weeks accordingly. That contextual judgment will not come from any tool.

## 3. Reading lists and source verification

Constructing a reading list is itself a pedagogical decision. Determining which sources the student will encounter, in what order, and toward what purpose is a methodological choice. The same discipline that source selection requires in research applies equally in teaching.

AI can propose a reading list. It can enumerate foundational works in a field, suggest thematic or chronological ordering, and identify sources that represent competing perspectives. These suggestions can save time and bring overlooked works into view. But every title on that list must be independently verified.

The risk of fabricated sources applies in teaching contexts just as it does in research. A model can produce plausible-looking titles, author names, and publication years. A wrong or non-existent entry on a list provided to students is not merely an academic error: it breaks the student's search process, wastes their time, and damages their trust in the course.

For this reason, every source must be verified independently — through a DOI, a publisher page, or a reliable library record. For sources without a persistent digital identifier, JSTOR, Google Scholar, or an institutional catalog serves as the confirmation point. This verification is the exact counterpart of citation hygiene in research and demands the same care. A source given to students that has not passed through this process represents an incomplete exercise of teaching responsibility.

## 4. Rubric design

A rubric is the visible, concrete form of assessment fairness. It shows students in advance what each criterion means, what level of performance meets it, and how scores are derived. A well-constructed rubric enables the student to work with expectations rather than uncertainty.

AI is quite effective at generating rubric drafts. Describe an assignment's purpose and it can separate out dimensions, write performance levels for each, and distribute points. This draft removes the most time-consuming first step in rubric development.

What must come from the instructor is the relationship between each criterion and the course's learning objectives, how students will actually be assessed, and — where criteria differ in importance — how weighting will be applied. Sadler (1989), in his foundational work on evaluative criteria, shows that a rubric is not merely a scoring instrument: well-defined criteria also function as a teaching tool that develops the student's capacity to evaluate their own work. For this function to be realized, criteria must be written in language through which the student can genuinely assess their own argument.

A rubric is not a mechanism for penalizing students. Its purpose is to make expectations visible from the start and to enable students to review their own work against those expectations before assessment. An AI-generated draft fulfills this purpose only when the instructor verifies both technical accuracy and pedagogical appropriateness separately.

## 5. Giving feedback on student writing

Feedback marks one of the most critical points in a student's development. But providing effective feedback is a process that demands time and attention. Reading the research drafts of thirty students in a single sitting and writing a meaningful comment on each is a genuine burden that most instructors face.

AI can ease this burden to a degree. It can serve as a functional tool for making feedback texts clearer, more constructive, and more readable. It can take a draft comment and restate it in more systematic language, render vague expressions concrete, and improve tonal consistency.

Carless (2006), examining feedback perception from both instructor and student perspectives, shows that how a comment is delivered matters as much as its content. If a student cannot use the feedback as a guide for the next stage of their work — if they see only what they produced rather than what to do next — feedback does not convert into a learning opportunity. Winstone and Carless (2019), in their extensive study of effective feedback processes, emphasize that feedback must be designed as a learning resource that the student actively uses, not a verdict they passively receive.

The practical conclusion from this line of work is clear. AI can refine the language of feedback; the academic judgment that determines its substance — which part of the student's argument is coherent, where depth is lacking, and what the next draft should focus on — belongs to the instructor. Feedback must not rewrite the student's text for them. Its function is to help the student see the next step.

There is an additional constraint that must be observed: student writing cannot be submitted to a third-party AI tool without written consent and institutional authorization. Original student text may fall within the scope of personal data. Institutional policy and student disclosure must be addressed explicitly before any tool is used.

## 6. Thesis advising

Thesis advising is the longest and most intensive educational relationship an instructor forms with a student. Several points at which AI intersects with this relationship carry their own specific limits.

Mapping the literature is among the most time-consuming tasks in the early stages of a thesis. A model can quickly list foundational works in a relevant field, suggest thematic groupings, and signal gaps. It can also assist with constructing a chapter outline, visualizing argument flow, and testing the logical consistency of a methodology section.

But the advisor's academic judgment cannot be substituted by any tool. Evaluating whether a research question makes an original contribution to the field, determining the acceptability of methodological choices, tracking the student's intellectual development, and forming a scholarly view on the thesis's defensibility are functions that belong to the advisor. These functions emerge with the process, not merely from its products.

The student's relationship with AI also merits separate attention. A student who presents AI-assisted content as their own work has not only crossed the boundary of academic integrity but has also shortened their own thesis process in ways that will matter later. The student must learn to pass whatever AI produces through their own critical filter. Boud and Molloy (2013), in their critique of feedback models oriented toward learning, point to this indirectly but with force: the student develops when they are the active subject of the process. If AI-generated content eliminates that agency, the tool has become an obstacle rather than an aid.

## 7. The limits of clinical supervision

Clinical supervision is the most sensitive domain within teaching. The material shared in supervision is by its nature private and confidential: a client case note, the content of a session, an assessment of a client, the difficulty an intern is navigating in training. Transferring any of this material to an AI tool, without prior anonymization, is simply not an option.

The anonymization process itself is insufficient when done carelessly. Removing names and institutional references is often not enough. Combined information that could identify the individual — age, gender, occupation, or contextual details through which a person could be identified without a name — must also be stripped out.

This limit cannot be relaxed on the grounds of educational purpose. The justification "I am using it to teach" does not alter confidentiality obligations. Clinical supervision material is privileged. Before any portion of a supervision session or case note is passed to an AI tool, it must be rendered anonymous. This requirement sits at the intersection of professional ethics codes, national data protection legislation, and institutional policy.

Supervision is one of the domains where confidentiality responsibility is most acutely felt. Carrying that responsibility means closing off shortcuts in tool use.

## 8. AI suspicion and fair assessment

AI detectors have moved to the center of the academic integrity debate in recent years. Did a student's submitted text come from an AI? Tools designed to answer that question proliferated quickly. But the technical limitations of these tools have become a genuine problem of assessment fairness.

Liang and colleagues (2023) demonstrated empirically that GPT detectors carry a systematic bias against writers whose first language is not English. Short sentence structures, transparent syntax, and limited vocabulary — features that characterize the natural writing style of many students — lead to false-positive classifications. A student writing in English from a Turkish, Arabic, or Chinese language background may be flagged unjustly simply because of their writing style, not because they used AI.

Accusing a student on the basis of a detector output alone is not fair. To be robust enough to handle AI suspicion justly, the assessment process should include: evidence of the writing process — a draft history or version record; an oral defense or face-to-face discussion, a setting in which the student is tested on whether they genuinely understand what they submitted; and a pedagogical conversation, meaning open-ended questions designed to follow the student's reasoning. Suspicion requires conversation before it can justify a consequence. This principle is both ethically necessary and the foundation of an institutionally defensible assessment practice.

## 9. Course-level AI policy

Every course should contain an explicit policy on AI use. Its absence creates an ambiguous space in which students do not know what is expected of them. That ambiguity exposes students to ethical errors and exposes instructors to inconsistent evaluation processes.

A course policy should answer the following questions. Which uses of AI are permitted: brainstorming for drafts, grammar checking, searching for sources? Which are restricted: text assistance with appropriate disclosure? Which are prohibited: having AI produce the substance of an assignment? How will disclosure be made: a standard acknowledgment template for each task, or an explicit note in the methods section?

These decisions vary with the nature of the course, the type of assessment, and institutional policy. A reflective journal and an analytical essay need not carry the same limits. What matters is that the boundary not remain unclear. UNESCO (2023), in its guidance on generative AI in education and research, emphasizes that clear policy is an institutional necessity at the course level. That expectation reaches down to individual courses.

Policy should explain learning responsibility rather than lead with punishment. Being able to say to students "in this course we use this tool in this way, and here is why" — rather than "if you do this, this will happen" — makes policy a pedagogical instrument rather than a disciplinary one.

## 10. Skill outputs

The `/teaching-feedback-ai-boundaries` skill directly supports the workflows addressed in this booklet. For syllabus drafting, it offers a starting scaffold for producing course outlines and planning reading list verification steps. For assessment design, it can assist with generating assignment rubric drafts and separating performance dimensions and evaluative criteria. For feedback practice, it can help refine the language of written comments toward a more constructive and readable register. For course governance, it can produce template language for a course-level AI use policy and for the boundaries to be communicated to students.

The skill operationalizes the central claim of this booklet. AI in teaching is a productivity tool, but for it to become a pedagogical responsibility tool, the user must maintain a deliberate relationship with it at every point. The tool acquires meaning in the hands of an instructor who knows what it can facilitate, what it cannot take on, and where the student's learning process must be protected from it.
