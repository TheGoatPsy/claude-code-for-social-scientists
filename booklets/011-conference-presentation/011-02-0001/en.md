# Public Scholarship, Blog, Press, and Social Media

The previous booklet examined the three principal formats a researcher faces when taking their work into a conference room: slides, a poster, and a lightning talk. This booklet addresses a different transition — the moment when academic knowledge leaves the journal page and travels beyond the conference room entirely, into the public arena. A blog post, a press release, a newspaper column, and a social media thread are all surfaces on which research conducts a dialogue with audiences who read under entirely different conditions than peer reviewers do.

This transition is less innocent than it appears. Academic text reaches its audience through the filter of peer review. Public text arrives without one. That directness amplifies both the opportunity and the risk. A finding can be misunderstood, exaggerated, or stripped of its context. The speed of social media carries a claim to thousands of people in seconds — and opens the same claim to misreading just as fast.

The central argument of this booklet is the following: simplifying research does not reduce the responsibility that comes with it. It transfers that responsibility into a more legible language. The ability to write good public scholarship is an extension of academic authorship and demands the same ethical discipline. Artificial intelligence is a powerful instrument in this translation. But — especially when mental health and vulnerable communities are involved — the boundaries must be drawn more precisely, and every adaptation step must include its own check on source fidelity and ethical language.

## 1. Academic Text and Public Text

An academic text speaks to a specialist reader — someone who shares a methodological language, a common conceptual framework, and a set of epistemic standards that have been established by the field. Public text speaks to people with different levels of knowledge, different reading habits, and different motivations. Some of them know nothing about the researcher's field. Some will read only the headline. Some will arrive carrying a personal experience related to the finding and will engage with the text, sometimes to agree, sometimes to push back.

This difference does not lower the standard of accuracy. It changes only the form of the telling. A good public text simplifies the research claim without pruning it. It does not deny complexity; it organizes complexity into a form the reader can carry. The difference between framing a finding as a "major breakthrough" and presenting it as "a notable contribution to this field" is not merely rhetorical. It is an epistemic choice.

The National Academies of Sciences, Engineering, and Medicine (2017) emphasizes that communicating science effectively with the public is not a technical skill alone. It requires managing trust, accuracy, and accessibility simultaneously. The first question a researcher should carry into public writing is this: what will someone who reads this understand about my findings — and how well does that understanding map onto what the research actually claims?

## 2. Simplifying and Diluting

The most common trap researchers fall into in public communication is confusing simplification with dilution. They are different things. Simplifying means removing unnecessary technical load, increasing intelligibility, and not leaving the reader stranded inside unfamiliar jargon. Diluting means erasing the boundaries of the claim, loading findings with a certainty they do not have, or concealing methodological limitations.

Fischhoff (2013) argues that science communication should be treated as a scientific discipline in its own right. The implication that follows: effective communication is not about making the message simpler — it is about structuring knowledge appropriately by keeping the recipient's needs and capacity in view. The same finding requires a different frame for a journalist, for a policy maker, and for a general reader. Each frame must preserve the accuracy boundary.

AI tools are both useful and risk-bearing at this junction. A model can rapidly adapt academic prose for different target audiences. But models tend to produce fluent, confident, and smooth text. The phrase "research shows that" used without a source does not establish a scientific fact — it creates an impression of vague authority. For every adaptation, the researcher should ask: what claim does this sentence actually make, and does the boundary of that claim appear in the text?

## 3. Mental Health Content and Clinical Responsibility

Communicating mental health research to the public carries an ethical weight that has no equivalent in most other fields. People who read these texts are often not seeking abstract information — they are trying to make sense of something they or someone close to them is living through. A reader working through a depression study to understand themselves or a loved one will readily translate clinical terminology into their own situation. That translation can be empowering. It can also mislead.

Four primary risks can be identified for mental health content in public communication. The first is using descriptive language in a way that invites the reader to self-diagnose. The second is presenting therapeutic interventions as a "miracle solution" or a "method that works for everyone." The third is using language that stigmatizes or blames specific groups or behaviors. The fourth is leaving a reader alone in a crisis moment — that is, failing to direct them toward professional support when the content calls for it.

For a researcher who is also a clinical psychologist writing public text, this responsibility sits heavier still. A public text can inform a reader, but it cannot position itself as a substitute for therapy. It can shed light on a reader's situation, but it cannot substitute for diagnosis. "Consider seeking a psychological counseling service" is a more honest and more directive phrase than the vague "get help." That distinction may look small on the page. In practice, it can be the difference between a reader reaching real support or not.

## 4. Not Generalizing from Individual Experience

One of the most frequent errors in public communication is presenting a single experience, a case study, or the finding of a small sample as if it were a universal rule. A researcher framing their own observation in the "research shows that" format crosses the line of academic honesty. Public text, when it encounters that format, assumes there is a broad, replicable, and systematic body of evidence behind it.

Peters (2013) demonstrates that the transfer of knowledge between science and media is an asymmetric process. A scientist speaks with full awareness of sources, methodological limits, and confidence intervals. Media and readers typically do not see those limits. That asymmetry means the researcher's responsibility in public text is to make methodological constraints visible rather than smooth them over. "This study was conducted on a sample of 120 people" sets a limit on the findings — and simultaneously builds credibility. The limit and the trustworthiness are the same sentence.

Personal narrative is one of the most powerful persuasive tools in social science communication. But the line between a singular experience and a generalizable finding must always be held. When that balance is in place, a text can be warm and rigorous at the same time. These qualities do not exclude each other. An effective public text carries both precisely because it has earned the right to do so.

## 5. Channel Differences and Content Adaptation

The same research taking different forms across different channels reflects the fact that each channel has its own reader expectations and its own communicative logic. A blog post accommodates a longer argument — the reader is there to learn, and they will follow the development of a point. A press release wants a clear central idea, a striking headline, and short paragraphs. A newspaper story reaches a reader who is rarely a specialist in the field and will form a first impression within thirty seconds of beginning.

Bubela and colleagues (2009) argue that science communication needs to be reconsidered — that the field should move from one-directional knowledge transfer to two-way dialogue. The practical consequence of that argument is this: when a researcher writes public text, they are not only asking "what am I saying" but also "what does the reader in this channel want to hear, and how do I build a bridge between that want and the truth?"

LinkedIn speaks to a professional and academic audience. That platform can accommodate methodological detail; findings can be stated more directly. Instagram calls for a visual memory and short attention — the same finding has to become three sentences, one slide, one image. The local press speaks to community context. Carrying a piece of research from Komotini into a local newspaper means using conditions the reader already recognizes, rather than a national reference frame — and that local grounding provides a far stronger foundation for the findings than any imported framework could. The academic core is reconstructed in each channel. It is not copied.

## 6. Adapting Public Text with AI

Claude Code handles certain tasks in this process quite well. It can convert academic prose into blog register, rebuild a technical paragraph in plain language, compress a five-section paper into a three-sentence social media post. Its speed and capacity for draft generation are real.

But there are three layers the researcher must audit in every adaptation. The first is source fidelity: does the text the model has produced match the claim of the original study? A model can produce a fluent but incorrect attribution, generate a statistic that does not exist, or merge two separate findings into one sentence. The second is the boundary of the claim: models tend to fill in uncertainty. "Evidence on this question is limited" can become "research shows that." The third is ethical language: in mental health content especially, the words a model selects may carry stigmatizing, exaggerated, or clinically inaccurate implications.

Treise and Weigold (2002) define the role of science communicators not as neutral conduits of information but as mediators who construct meaning across different contexts. From that frame, AI produces drafts — but meaning construction and responsibility belong to the researcher. Claude Code is a tool. What is done with that tool, whose voice speaks through it, and within which ethical boundary the work remains — those are the author's decisions.

## 7. Vulnerable Communities and Local Context

Writing public text about minority communities, people with migration experience, clinical patient populations, and small settlement communities places a heightened representational responsibility on the researcher. That responsibility has two dimensions: accuracy and respect.

On the accuracy side: findings about these communities often carry methodological constraints — incomplete sampling, limited geographic access, or measurement instruments not normed for these groups. Those constraints must be made visible in public text. Left invisible, a finding reinforces a pattern that misrepresents the community it claims to describe.

On the respect side: identity language must be chosen with care. For a researcher working in or carrying the context of Western Thrace into public writing, this sensitivity is concrete. The community should be conveyed in its own terms of self-identification rather than through externally imposed labels. Personal details the reader might recognize are removed when they are not necessary. Representation is built in the space between visibility and privacy. That balance does not compromise the credibility of the research — it strengthens it.

## 8. Source Fidelity and the Public Text Archive

A public text need not carry a bibliography. Adding six references to a blog post unsettles the reader and breaks the flow. But source fidelity is a responsibility that exists independently of a bibliography. The researcher must be able to see, in their own archive, which finding came from which study.

This archival practice serves several important functions. First, if a claim made in public text is later questioned, the researcher has a reliable foundation. Second, it makes it easier to detect fabricated or misattributed citations that have crept into AI-generated text. Third, when the same research is adapted for multiple channels, the archive ensures all versions remain attached to the same scientific core.

The study's DOI, its full citation, and a brief note recording which finding was carried into the public text together constitute a vital safeguard. The attitude of "I know this without a reference" leads over time to a blurring of which claim came from which study. That blurring accelerates when AI adaptation is involved, because the model does not hold a stable distinction between what has been verified and what it has generated.

## 9. Disclosure and Transparency

The obligations of academic integrity follow public text even when the form changes. When a researcher uses AI assistance to draft a blog post or prepare social media content, how do they disclose that use? No universal standard has yet settled. But the direction is clear.

In academic and institutional contexts, disclosure should be explicit. A research summary published on a university website or an institutional social media post can carry a brief disclosure note at the bottom or in a transparency statement. On personal social media accounts, the essential practice is not a formal disclosure form but maintaining the line of source and responsibility. Saying "this finding comes from study X" is not a bibliography note — it is part of the trust relationship built with the reader.

Disclosure does not weaken the text. It demonstrates that the researcher is in command of the production process, using a tool without being directed by it. Explaining the limits of AI involvement, rather than concealing any involvement at all, protects the long-term trust relationship with both the reader and the academic community.

## 10. The Value of Quality Public Scholarship for the Researcher

Public communication is frequently perceived by researchers as an additional burden — a responsibility that sits alongside the real work, one that can be deferred or dropped. That perception is becoming less accurate both in how research is evaluated and in how science maintains its relationship with society.

When research findings do not reach the public, three things happen. Policy makers make decisions in fields touching on the researcher's work without hearing the researcher's voice. Inaccurate or exaggerated accounts of the field spread in public discourse, while the researcher's capacity to correct them remains limited. And public trust in research erodes — because the impression that research stays behind closed doors feeds skepticism.

Quality public communication extends the reach of research. It is possible to see this not as an academic obligation but as part of the researcher's social contract. AI can shorten preparation time, generate drafts, and clear language barriers in this process. But the core work remains in the researcher's hands: deciding which finding to carry, in which voice, within which boundary, and to which reader.
