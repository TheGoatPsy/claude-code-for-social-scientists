# Slides, Posters, and Lightning Talks with AI Assistance

The previous booklet returned the revised manuscript to its own voice, and the article came out the other side of peer review. The work's next stop is a room. Conference presentation lives in three different compressions of the same study: the slides of a twenty-minute talk, one square meter of poster, and a three-minute lightning talk. All three ask the same question. When the essence of this study is fitted to this duration and this surface, what should remain? This booklet covers the discipline of the three forms, the rules of visual integrity, and a bilingual production line with Claude Code, with disclosure awareness riding along the whole way.

## 1. The Conference Trio

The three forms look like the same material at different scales, and that resemblance is exactly why they get confused. A talk lets an argument develop. The audience stays with you for twenty minutes and consents to a flow. A poster is a waypoint. The colleague walking past decides in about three seconds whether to stop. A lightning talk is a memorability test for a single claim. Resizing one slide deck for all three forms is the shortest route to failing at all three.

The right starting point is the shared core. The study's one-sentence claim, its one figure, and its one takeaway are identical across the forms. The forms wrap different amounts of flesh around that core. No form is started before the core is fixed.

## 2. The Reality of the Bilingual Presentation

A social scientist working in Türkiye usually presents the same study in two different rooms, in Turkish at the national congress and in English at the international conference. Amano and colleagues (2023) documented, in a multi-country survey, the extra burden that scientists whose first language is not English carry across scientific activities, and the burden does not stop at writing papers. Preparing and giving a presentation in English costs additional time and additional anxiety for anyone working in a second language.

The parity discipline from the writing booklet steps onto the stage here. Two decks are produced from one outline. Section order, claims, and figures are paired; the sentences are rebuilt in the target language. Slide text translated word for word betrays itself the moment it is read aloud in the room. Shared outline, native language. The principle is the same on stage as it is on paper.

## 3. Slide Discipline

A slide has a listener, not a reader, and the discipline follows from that difference. Naegle (2021), in her rules for effective presentation slides, distributes the load according to the audience's attention. One slide carries one message. The title is not a topic label but a claim sentence. The text is not a copy of what the speaker will say but visual support for it. Every extra line written onto a slide closes the listener's ears and opens their eyes, and the speaker starts competing with their own deck.

At sentence level the measure is the same as in writing. The principle of Williams and Bizup (2016) applies to slide headlines too: actor in the subject, action in the verb. "The relationship between social anxiety and digital self-presentation" is a label. "As social anxiety rises, self-presentation narrows" is a claim, and the listener remembers the second.

## 4. Poster Discipline

Honesty about posters comes first. Rowe and Ilic (2009), in a pilot survey of author attitudes and experiences on the question of what impact posters have on academic knowledge transfer, found that authors perceive that impact as limited. A poster is not a publication channel in its own right. The right design goal follows. A poster is not an article summary. It is a conversation starter.

The starter's rules are short. One finding sentence readable from two meters. One strong figure at the center. A QR code that hands the full text to whoever wants the detail. A surface that communicates even in the minutes when the author is not standing next to it. In a crowded poster hall, what turns a three-second glance into a conversation is not density. It is clarity.

## 5. The Lightning Talk

The three-minute format is the most unforgiving compression in academic storytelling, and it has its own cutting order. Methods detail falls first, then the literature frame, then the secondary findings. The trio that remains is the whole format. Why the problem matters, what the single finding is, and which sentence the listener should leave the room with.

The format is also an opportunity, because the single-claim discipline tests the core of the other two forms. A study that cannot be told in three minutes is, most of the time, a study that has not yet found its one sentence. Lightning-talk preparation therefore belongs at the beginning of talk preparation, not at its end.

## 6. Visual Integrity

Presentation visuals circulate with less scrutiny than article figures, and for exactly that reason they demand stricter self-discipline. Rougier, Droettboom, and Bourne (2014), in their rules for better figures, state the heaviest one plainly: a figure must not mislead its reader. Truncated axes, manipulated scales, and selectively chosen ranges are relatives of data fraud in the seminar room as much as in print.

AI-assisted production adds a new item to that list. A schematic the model drew and a chart produced from data are not the same kind of object, and on a slide they look identical. The rule is sharp. An illustrative visual says it is illustrative. A data visual is generated from real data, and the code that generated it lives in the archive. A beautiful chart of an analysis that does not exist is an integrity violation in even the smallest room.

## 7. A Production Line with Claude Code

The production line is the writing scaffold adapted for the stage. Given the article's outline, the model drafts the skeleton of all three forms: a slide flow with a claim headline per slide for the talk, a zone layout for the poster, a three-sentence spine for the lightning talk. Speaker notes derive from the same outline, and in bilingual production every skeleton is rebuilt inside the target language.

The boundary does not move. The truth of the claim headlines, the choice of the finding, and the placement of emphasis belong to the researcher. The model speeds up the skeleton; the sentence that will be spoken on stage is written by its owner. When the deck is finished, the parity check runs. Do the two languages' decks carry the same claims, in the same order, with the same figures?

## 8. Disclosure Awareness

The disclosure norms of the presentation world are younger than those of publishing, and they vary from venue to venue. The principle still follows use. Hosseini, Rasmussen, and Resnik (2023) tie the duty of disclosure to the use itself, and the line holds on stage. If the deck was prepared with AI assistance and the conference provides a disclosure field, the field gets filled. If there is no field and the question comes, the answer is plain: the skeleton and the language polish were assisted, the content and the claims are mine.

The stage has one more honesty boundary of its own. In the room, results you do not have are not presented. An ongoing analysis is presented as ongoing, and a projection is not dressed up as a finding. The presentation is ephemeral, but the listener's memory and the photographed slide are not.

## 9. Rehearsal and Question Preparation

The most valuable form of rehearsal is the one in which the model plays the audience. Hand over the deck and make a single request: which questions would a rigorous colleague watching this talk ask? The returned list is the map of preparation. The methods question, the limitation question, and the so-what question arrive in some form in every room.

A three-question drill is enough for most speakers. A one-minute answer is prepared for each and rehearsed aloud. The question without an answer is part of the preparation too. A speaker who has rehearsed saying we do not know that yet is the most trustworthy person in the room.

## 10. Bridge, When Things Go Wrong on Stage

A presentation is a live performance, and live performance is the natural habitat of this guide's closing booklet. The projector refuses, the deck opens in an old version, the time slot is cut in half. The troubleshooting protocol of the final booklet applies to these stage moments as much as to tool failures. A backup plan, a local copy, and a one-page summary are the presenter's insurance. When things go wrong, moving with a method beats panic on stage too.
