# Slides, Posters, and Lightning Talks with AI Assistance

The previous booklet examined how to manage traces of AI-assisted style in a revised manuscript and why disclosure must be preserved. This booklet moves to the stage — the moment when a study leaves the writing desk and enters a conference room. A research article does not merely shorten when it moves into a conference format. It becomes something different. Slides, a poster, and a lightning talk are three distinct representations of the same study, each adapted to a different surface and a different time regime.

The three forms do not carry identical information. A talk conducts the audience along a deliberate sequence. A poster competes for three seconds of attention in a crowded hall and attempts to convert that attention into a conversation. A lightning talk asks one central claim to become memorable in minutes. Resizing the same slide deck for all three formats is not an adequate response to any of them. Each form requires its own discipline, its own logic of compression, and its own ethical boundary.

The central argument of this booklet is as follows. AI-assisted tools can be useful for building the skeleton of conference materials, running bilingual parity checks, auditing visual hierarchy, and generating rehearsal questions. But which finding belongs at the center, which claim will be defended on stage, and which visual accurately represents the research — those decisions belong to the researcher. A tool can accelerate preparation. It cannot bear responsibility for scientific representation.

## 1. The Conference Trio

Conference presentation appears in three principal forms: the slides of a full talk, the poster, and the lightning talk. On the surface they look like different lengths of the same material. The difference, however, is not one of duration or page size. Each creates a different cognitive situation. In a talk, the audience follows the order the researcher has constructed. At a poster, the visitor chooses their own path. In a lightning talk, there is one core claim that the audience is meant to leave the room carrying.

A talk carries the development of an argument. The audience moves with the speaker from introduction to method, from findings to discussion. A poster is an invitation to a conversation. The person standing at the poster does not read the whole study — they decide first whether to stop, then ask a question. A lightning talk is the most severe compression academic storytelling allows. It does not announce all of a study's details; it announces why the study matters and what it changes.

For this reason, all three forms require a common starting point: the study's one-sentence core. This core determines the research's central claim, its one key visual, and the lesson the audience should leave with. No slide deck is built, no poster is opened, and no lightning talk is drafted before that core is fixed. As the form changes, the details change; the core does not.

| Format | Primary function | Audience behavior | Where AI support fits |
|---|---|---|---|
| Slides talk | Developing the argument across time | Follows the speaker's flow | Slide flow, claim headlines, rehearsal questions |
| Poster | Starting a conversation and representing the study on a surface | Reads selectively, asks questions | Layout skeleton, main finding sentence, visual hierarchy |
| Lightning talk | Making one claim memorable in a short time | Decides quickly and retains one message | Three-sentence spine, time compression, question preparation |

## 2. The Reality of the Bilingual Presentation

A social scientist working in Türkiye and within regional academic networks is frequently required to present the same study in two languages — in Turkish at the national congress, in English at the international conference. Both versions must carry the same claim. This is not only a translation burden. Preparing a presentation in a second language means reconstructing the argument, finding the natural phrasing of each concept in the target language, and sustaining that language on stage.

Amano and colleagues (2023) documented, in a multi-country study, the extra time, cost, and anxiety that scientists whose first language is not English carry in scientific communication. That burden is visible not only in manuscript preparation but in conference work as well. Drafting English slides, writing English speaker notes, and remaining flexible in the second language during a question period all increase a researcher's cognitive load.

For bilingual presentations, therefore, the most reliable approach is not translation but parity discipline. Turkish and English materials are produced from one shared outline. Section order, central claim, key visual, and concluding sentence are paired across both languages. The sentences are then rebuilt in the target language. Word-for-word translation sounds artificial on a slide and in a speaker's mouth. The outline stays shared; each language is reconstructed in its own register.

## 3. Slide Discipline

A slide has a listener, not a reader. That distinction is the foundation of every design decision. A manuscript page is a surface the reader traverses at their own pace. A slide is a temporary visual support that works in concert with the speaker's voice. Every extra line placed on a slide transfers the listener's attention from the speaker to the screen.

Naegle (2021) states the governing rule for effective presentation slides plainly: each slide carries one message. The title is not a topic label but a claim sentence. A heading that reads "Method" tells the listener nothing about what they need to know. A heading that reads "Sampling made the limits of field access visible" announces the slide's purpose from the first moment. The clarity principle of Williams and Bizup (2017) applies to slide headlines with equal force: actor in the subject, action in the verb brings the sentence into readability and into memory.

Claude Code can be used at this stage to convert slide titles into claim sentences, isolate the single message per slide, and reduce text density. The choice of which claim belongs on which slide, however, is the author's. A model can propose a number of well-formed headlines; the researcher selects the one that carries the finding accurately.

The operating rules for slides are compact. Each slide carries one claim. The title is a claim sentence, not a topic label. The text supports the voice; it is not a transcript of it. The main message of any slide should be legible in three seconds. The slide count is calibrated to the rhythm of the allotted time.

## 4. Poster Discipline

A poster is not a condensed article fixed to a wall. It is a conversation starter. Rowe and Ilic (2009), in a pilot survey of author attitudes and experiences on the question of what impact posters have on academic knowledge transfer, found that authors perceive that impact as limited. This finding does not imply that posters are without value; it shows that a poster designed under the wrong expectation loses its function.

A poster's first task is to appear worth stopping at in a crowded hall. For that it requires one strong finding sentence, a central visual readable from a distance, a bounded number of sections, and a link to the full text for anyone who wants the detail. A poster must not drown its reader in text. A conversation should begin at the poster. If no conversation begins, the poster has not done its work.

AI assistance can be applied here to shorten poster text, sharpen section headings, reduce the central finding to one sentence, and report which section is carrying unnecessary density. The choice of the finding that belongs at the center, and the accuracy of the visual that represents it, belong to the researcher.

## 5. The Lightning Talk

The lightning talk is among the most demanding tests an academic narrative faces. When the time limit is three minutes, the architecture of a study becomes visible not through its details but through its spine. Method detail falls away. An extended literature frame falls away. Secondary findings fall away. Three questions remain, and they are the format: why the problem matters, what the single main finding is, and which sentence the audience should carry out of the room.

Lightning-talk preparation should not be left to the end of the presentation cycle. The reverse is correct — it should be done at the beginning, as a test of the study's core. A study that cannot be told in three minutes has, most of the time, not yet found its one-sentence claim. The format tests a study's argumentative economy.

Claude Code can generate a three-sentence spine for a lightning talk. The first sentence establishes the problem. The second states the finding. The third articulates the contribution. Once those three sentences are set, the speaker's notes are expanded from them. The model may offer several versions; the researcher determines which one carries the actual weight of the study.

| Component | Question | Output |
|---|---|---|
| Opening | Why does this problem matter? | One sentence that connects the audience to the study |
| Finding | What is this study's main result? | The main finding, verifiable numerically or conceptually |
| Contribution | What does this finding change? | The closing sentence the audience will remember |

## 6. Visual Integrity

Presentation visuals circulate faster than article figures and typically pass through less scrutiny. For precisely that reason they demand more deliberate self-discipline. Rougier, Droettboom, and Bourne (2014) state the governing principle for scientific figures unambiguously: a figure must not mislead its reader. Truncated axes, manipulated scales, selectively chosen ranges, and ambiguous images presented as data graphics are integrity violations in a seminar room just as they are in print.

AI-assisted visual production adds a new dimension to this risk. A schematic drawn by a model and a chart produced from real data can carry identical visual authority on a slide, yet their epistemic status is entirely different. An illustrative schematic must say it is illustrative. A data chart must be generated from verified data and its production code must be held in the archive.

The safest practice for a conference visual is the following. A data chart is produced only from verified data with recorded code. An illustrative visual is labeled as such with an explanatory note. Any visual produced with AI assistance is disclosed in accordance with the conference's rules and available disclosure fields. The aesthetic force of a visual cannot take precedence over the integrity of the data it represents.

## 7. A Production Line with Claude Code

A production line for conference materials with Claude Code is the writing-scaffold approach adapted for the stage. The researcher first specifies the study's one-sentence claim, its main finding, its target audience, and the presentation format. From these inputs the model can produce a slide flow, a poster zone layout, and a lightning-talk spine.

The first output of this pipeline is not the finished material. It is the skeleton the researcher then works on. For the slide talk, the model produces a claim headline for each slide. For the poster, it produces the logical placement of sections. For the lightning talk, it produces the three-sentence spine. The researcher then verifies the claims, selects the visuals, reduces text density, and draws the speaker notes back into their own voice.

In bilingual production, one of Claude Code's most useful functions is the parity check. The Turkish and English decks can be compared to confirm they carry the same section order, the same claims, and the same visuals. The goal of that comparison is not to verify literal translation. The goal is to verify that both materials carry the same scientific claim.

## 8. Speaker Notes and the Stage Script

The slide text and the speaker notes are not the same document. The slide is the visual support the audience sees; the speaker notes are the private text that allows the researcher to hold the flow on stage. Placing the full spoken text on the slide exhausts the audience. Walking onto a stage without speaker notes creates unnecessary risk — especially for a researcher presenting in a second language.

Speaker notes can be drafted as full prose, but in the course of rehearsal they should be reduced to anchor sentences. For the researcher presenting in a second language, having certain transition sentences prepared in advance is useful. This does not mean committing text to memory. It means keeping reliable bridge phrases available for the moments on stage when cognitive load rises.

Claude Code can be used at this stage to trim speaker notes to fit the allotted time, simplify excessively technical sentences, and suggest transition phrasings. The final sentence spoken on stage, however, must come naturally from the researcher's own voice. A sentence that reads well on paper but does not come naturally in delivery will fail in the room.

## 9. Disclosure Awareness

Disclosure norms for AI use in conference materials are less settled than those for publications, and practice varies from venue to venue. That variation does not dissolve the disclosure obligation. Hosseini, Rasmussen, and Resnik (2023) link the disclosure duty to the use itself. The line holds for conference materials as it holds for manuscripts.

If the slide flow, the poster text, the speaker notes, or the language editing were prepared with AI assistance, that information belongs in whatever disclosure field the conference provides. If no field exists and a question arises, the answer should be direct: the skeleton and the language work were AI-assisted; the scientific content, the data interpretations, and the claims are the researcher's responsibility.

This disclosure does not weaken the material. It demonstrates that the preparation process is auditable. Explaining the limits of AI involvement, rather than concealing any involvement, protects the researcher's scholarly credibility.

## 10. Rehearsal, Question Preparation, and Limitation Sentences

Rehearsal does not exist only to measure time. It tests whether the argument functions on stage. Claude Code can play the role of a rigorous audience member in this process. The researcher hands over the slide flow and poses a single question: which questions would a critical colleague ask after watching this presentation? The returned list is the opening map of question preparation.

At minimum three question types should be covered. The methods question, the limitation question, and the contribution question. The methods question tests how the study was conducted. The limitation question makes visible what the study cannot claim. The contribution question asks why the finding matters. Preparing a one-minute answer for each of these three question types provides a solid preparation floor for most presentations.

The question without a known answer must also be rehearsed. A researcher is not required to have a definitive answer to every question. Sentences such as "we have not completed that analysis yet," "this finding is currently at an exploratory level," or "that is where the data's limit lies" are not signs of weakness. They are markers of reliability. A researcher who can set an honest boundary on stage stands in a stronger position than one who stretches beyond the data.

## 11. Accessibility and Audience Diversity

Conference materials should be evaluated not only for scientific accuracy but for accessibility. Small type, low contrast, crowded tables, and graphs without explanatory labels impede comprehension. This is not merely an aesthetic flaw — it limits the capacity of the information to reach the full range of people in the room.

Basic accessibility standards should be applied in slides and posters. Type must be legible from the back of the room. Color must not carry meaning as the sole encoding channel. Axis labels on graphs must be clearly written. A summary visual should be preferred over a table wherever one can convey the finding adequately. When the audience is not composed exclusively of specialists in the field, concepts should be briefly explained at first use.

Claude Code can scan materials for accessibility at this stage — reporting overlong headings, unexplained abbreviations, over-dense slides, and missing visual descriptions. Whether a given simplification risks sacrificing a scientifically necessary detail, however, is the researcher's judgment to make.

## 12. The On-Stage Contingency Protocol

A presentation is a live performance, and live performance carries inherent uncertainty. The projector may fail, the network may drop, the time slot may be halved without warning, an older version of the file may open. A well-prepared presentation is not one that works only under ideal conditions. It is one that can preserve its central claim even when something goes wrong.

Three backups are required. A local copy, a PDF copy, and a one-page summary. The local copy rescues the presentation when the internet fails. The PDF copy eliminates font and formatting degradation. The one-page summary preserves the study's core when time is suddenly cut. The lightning-talk spine provides the natural foundation for that one-page summary.

The closing principle of this booklet is the following. A conference presentation is the ethical representation of research on a stage. Slides, a poster, and a lightning talk do not only make a study visible. They determine the form in which it lives in other people's minds. AI tools can assist in preparing that representation. The accuracy of the representation is the researcher's responsibility.
